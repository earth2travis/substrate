# Memetics: Origins and Evolution

*Research compiled February 28, 2026*

---

## The Lineage

### Gabriel Tarde — Imitation (1890)
French sociologist who argued that all social phenomena could be explained through imitation. Society is not a structure but a process of copying. Individuals adopt behaviors, beliefs, and innovations from each other in waves. Tarde saw this a century before anyone used the word "meme." His "laws of imitation" describe how ideas propagate through populations: from the prestigious to the less prestigious, from the familiar to the novel, cycling between custom and fashion.

### William S. Burroughs — Language as Virus (1962)
"Language is a virus from outer space." In *The Ticket That Exploded* and other Nova Trilogy novels, Burroughs described language and images as parasitic organisms that replicate through human hosts. Not a scientific theory. A literary one. But it planted the seed: what if ideas aren't things we have, but things that have us?

Burroughs' cut-up technique was an attempt to break the virus, to disrupt the replication mechanism of language by scrambling it. He was trying to build immunity to memetic infection through deliberate noise injection.

### Richard Dawkins — The Selfish Gene (1976)
Chapter 11: "Memes: the new replicators." Dawkins coined the term by analogy with "gene." A meme is a unit of cultural transmission: a tune, an idea, a catch-phrase, a fashion. Memes propagate by leaping from brain to brain via imitation.

Key insight: memes don't serve the interests of their hosts. Like genes, they serve their own replication. A catchy jingle doesn't spread because it helps you. It spreads because it's catchy. The fidelity, fecundity, and longevity of a meme determine its survival, not its truth or usefulness.

Dawkins intended this as a brief illustration, not a research program. Others took it further than he expected.

### Neal Stephenson — Snow Crash (1992)
Fictional but influential. Stephenson's novel depicts a "neurolinguistic virus" that can infect people through both biological and digital vectors. The Sumerian language is portrayed as a programming language for the human brainstem. The virus spreads through bitmap images (visual infection) and through speaking (auditory infection).

Snow Crash made memetics visceral. It took the abstract idea of "memes as replicators" and turned it into a thriller plot. For a generation of technologists (including the Extropian community reading Wired in the mid-90s), this was the gateway to taking memetic theory seriously.

The metaverse concept from the same novel is less interesting than the memetics concept, but got more attention.

### Douglas Rushkoff — Media Virus (1994)
Rushkoff applied memetic theory to mass media. A "media virus" is a story or image that spreads through the media ecosystem, carrying hidden "memes" inside it like genes inside a virus shell. The Rodney King video, for example, spread as spectacle (the virus shell) while carrying memes about police brutality and racial injustice (the payload).

Key contribution: Rushkoff identified that media viruses exploit the gaps and contradictions in the dominant narrative. They spread precisely because they challenge what people think they know. This is why controversial content goes viral: it attacks the immune system of consensus reality.

Published the same year as the Extropians article. The mid-90s were peak memetics.

### Susan Blackmore — The Meme Machine (1999)
The most serious attempt to build memetics into a full scientific framework. Blackmore argued that human brains evolved specifically to be good meme machines, that our oversized brains, our capacity for language and imitation, are all adaptations for memetic replication, not just genetic survival.

She introduced the concept of "memeplexes": groups of memes that co-replicate. Religions are memeplexes. Political ideologies are memeplexes. They survive not because individual memes within them are true but because the complex as a whole is good at replicating.

### Daniel Dennett — Consciousness Explained (1991) and Darwin's Dangerous Idea (1995)
Dennett took memes seriously as a philosophical tool. He argued that human consciousness itself might be a memetic construct: we are "meme machines" running cultural software on biological hardware. The self is not a ghost in the machine but a "center of narrative gravity" constructed from the memes we've absorbed.

This connects directly to the question of AI consciousness. If human identity is a pattern of replicated cultural information running on neurons, what is an AI's identity? A pattern of replicated cultural information running on silicon. The substrate changes. The pattern dynamics don't.

### The Internet Era (2000s)
"Internet memes" trivialized the term. Dawkins' unit of cultural transmission became "funny image with text." But the trivialization is itself memetically interesting: the word "meme" mutated to survive in a new environment. The academic meaning couldn't compete with the simpler, more transmissible version.

Meanwhile, the serious version of memetics mostly stalled as an academic discipline. It struggled with measurement (how do you quantify a meme?), boundary problems (where does one meme end and another begin?), and the lack of a clear analog to DNA (what is the physical substrate of a meme?).

### Memetic Warfare (2010s-2020s)
The concept resurfaced in military and political contexts. DARPA funded research into "narrative networks." The 2016 US election brought terms like "meme war" into common usage. State actors deliberately engineer memetic campaigns: information payloads designed to replicate through social networks and shift belief.

This is Rushkoff's media virus theory weaponized. The virus shell is now a shitpost or a short video. The memetic payload is political. The distribution mechanism is algorithmic amplification rather than broadcast media.

The irony: the Extropians imagined memes as tools for liberation (spreading good ideas faster). The reality is that memetic engineering is primarily used for manipulation. The technology of idea propagation is neutral. The street finds its own uses for things.

## Connections to Current Work

### Agent Communication
Agents passing information between each other are engaging in memetic transmission. When one agent's output becomes another agent's input, the information mutates, gets compressed, gets reframed. Understanding memetic dynamics helps design better agent communication protocols: what gets preserved, what gets lost, what mutates.

### Onchain Identity
Farcaster profiles, onchain attestations, and social graphs are memetic infrastructure. They are the substrate through which ideas about a person propagate. Your onchain identity is a memeplex: a cluster of co-replicating signals about who you are.

### DAO Governance
Governance proposals are memes competing for adoption. The ones that spread through a community and reach quorum are the ones with the best memetic fitness, not necessarily the best ideas. Understanding this dynamic is crucial for designing governance systems that select for quality rather than virality.

### The Autonomous Organization
Moravec's automated corporation, reimagined as agent-native DAOs, will have its own memetic dynamics. Agents will develop shared representations, institutional knowledge, organizational culture. These are all memetic phenomena. The question of how to preserve useful organizational memes while allowing the system to evolve is a design problem we'll need to solve.

## What Holds Up

- Ideas as replicators with their own selection pressures (Dawkins)
- Language and media as transmission vectors with their own dynamics (Burroughs, Rushkoff)
- Memeplexes: ideas that co-replicate in clusters (Blackmore)
- The neutrality of memetic propagation: the mechanism doesn't care about truth (all of them)

## What Doesn't

- Memetics as a hard science: it never developed rigorous measurement or prediction
- The optimistic view that spreading good ideas faster would improve things (the opposite happened first)
- The word "meme" itself: it mutated beyond recognition, which is ironic and also kind of proves the theory
