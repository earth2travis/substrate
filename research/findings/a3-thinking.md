# A3 Thinking and Problem Solving

_Research completed 2026-02-02. Related: issue #51._

## Origins

A3 thinking traces back to Toyota in the decades following World War II, though the specific date of its emergence is difficult to pin down. Unlike many management tools that arrive with a named inventor and a publication date, the A3 report grew organically inside the Toyota Production System as a practical response to a practical problem: how do you get an entire organization to think clearly about problems and communicate that thinking without drowning in paperwork?

The name itself is mundane. A3 refers to the ISO 216 paper size, 297 millimeters by 420 millimeters, roughly equivalent to the American 11 by 17 inch tabloid sheet. Toyota engineers and managers began capturing their problem solving work on this single sheet of paper because it was the largest size that could be faxed (this was Japan in the 1960s and 1970s, after all). The constraint was initially technological, not philosophical. But it became philosophical. The discipline of fitting an entire investigation onto one page forced a kind of thinking that longer documents never demanded.

Taiichi Ohno, the architect of the Toyota Production System, championed genchi genbutsu: go and see for yourself. The A3 embodied this principle. You could not fill one out from a conference room. You had to go to where the work happened, observe the current condition, gather facts, and distill them into something tight enough to fit the paper. It was a thinking tool disguised as a document format.

John Shook, who became the first American manager at Toyota in Japan, brought the practice to Western audiences through his 2008 book _Managing to Learn_. Durward Sobek and Art Smalley published _Understanding A3 Thinking_ the same year. These books revealed what Toyota insiders had known for decades: the A3 was not about the paper. It was about developing people who could think systematically about problems and lead others through the logic of a solution.

## The Toyota Production System Context

To understand A3 thinking, you have to understand the system that produced it.

The Toyota Production System rests on two pillars. **Just in time** means producing only what is needed, when it is needed, in the amount needed. **Jidoka** means automation with a human touch, stopping the line when something goes wrong rather than letting defects propagate. Together these pillars create a culture where problems are not inconveniences to be hidden. They are signals. Every defect is information about where the system needs to improve.

This culture of surfacing problems required tools for working through them methodically. The A3 report became one of the most important of those tools, not because it was the most sophisticated, but because it was the most accessible. Any worker at any level could pick up a sheet of A3 paper and work through a problem using the structured sections. Any manager could read one and understand the full arc of investigation in minutes.

The A3 also served a deeper purpose inside Toyota: developing people. The lean leader's job, according to the Toyota philosophy, is to build people before building cars. When a manager assigns a subordinate to own an A3, the manager does not dictate the solution. The manager coaches by asking questions. "What did you actually observe?" "How do you know that is the root cause?" "What alternatives did you consider?" The A3 becomes a vehicle for teaching someone how to think, not just how to solve a particular problem.

This mentorship dimension is what separates A3 thinking from ordinary report writing. The document is secondary. The dialogue it generates is primary.

## The A3 Report Format

An A3 report follows a structured flow that moves from left to right across the page, mirroring the progression of thought from understanding a problem to implementing a solution. While templates vary across organizations, the canonical sections are consistent.

### Left Side: Understanding the Problem

The left half of the A3 focuses on grasping the situation. It answers the questions: What is happening? What should be happening? Why is there a gap?

**1. Title and Owner**

The top of the A3 carries a clear title describing the problem or initiative and the name (or initials) of the person who owns the investigation. Ownership matters. The A3 owner is the individual who has accepted responsibility for driving the problem to resolution. This person may not have authority over every aspect of the situation, but they are accountable for the thinking, the consensus building, and the follow through.

**2. Background**

This section provides the context a reader needs to understand why this problem matters. It answers: Why are we looking at this? What business need or organizational priority does this connect to? The background should be brief. Two or three sentences. Enough to establish relevance without becoming a history lesson.

**3. Current Condition**

This is the heart of the left side. It describes what is actually happening right now, based on direct observation and data, not assumptions or secondhand reports. Diagrams, process maps, data tables, and sketches are common here. The current condition should make the gap between what is and what should be immediately visible.

At Toyota, this section embodies genchi genbutsu. You cannot write it without going to the gemba (the place where work happens) and seeing the situation for yourself. This is one of the sections where people most often stumble. The temptation is to describe the problem as you imagine it. The discipline is to describe it as you observed it.

**4. Goal or Target Condition**

What does success look like? This section states the specific, measurable outcome you are working toward. Not a vague aspiration ("improve quality") but a concrete target ("reduce defect rate from 4.2% to 1.5% by Q3"). The gap between the current condition and the target condition defines the scope of the investigation.

**5. Root Cause Analysis**

Why does the gap exist? This section applies analytical methods to understand the underlying causes of the problem. The 5 Whys technique is the most common tool here, but fishbone diagrams (Ishikawa diagrams), Pareto analysis, and other methods are also used. The key discipline is to move beyond symptoms to systemic causes.

This section often connects directly to the 5 Whys research we completed previously (see issue #22). The A3 provides the container; the 5 Whys provides the analytical engine for drilling into causal chains.

### Right Side: Proposing and Implementing Solutions

The right half of the A3 shifts from understanding to action. It answers: What will we do about it? How? When? And did it work?

**6. Countermeasures**

Toyota uses the word "countermeasures" rather than "solutions" deliberately. A solution implies finality. A countermeasure implies a response to the current understanding of the problem, one that may need adjustment as learning continues. This section describes the specific actions proposed to address each root cause identified on the left side.

Good countermeasures are concrete, actionable, and clearly linked to the root causes they address. If a countermeasure cannot be traced back to a specific cause, it is likely a guess rather than a reasoned response.

**7. Implementation Plan**

Who will do what, by when? This section lays out the specific tasks, owners, and deadlines for putting countermeasures into effect. It often takes the form of a simple table or timeline. The implementation plan is where good intentions either become real changes or die quietly.

**8. Follow Up**

How will we know whether the countermeasures worked? This section defines the metrics, checkpoints, and review dates for evaluating results. It closes the loop. Without follow up, the A3 is an exercise in analysis without accountability.

Some organizations add a ninth element: **results** or **confirmation of effect**, which captures the actual outcomes after implementation and compares them to the target condition. This section transforms the A3 from a plan into a learning record.

## PDCA on a Single Page

The A3 report is often described as "PDCA on paper," and the mapping is direct.

**Plan** corresponds to the left side of the A3: understanding the current condition, defining the target, and analyzing root causes. This is the investigation phase. It also extends to the countermeasures section, where the proposed response is planned.

**Do** corresponds to the implementation plan: carrying out the countermeasures.

**Check** corresponds to the follow up section: measuring whether the countermeasures achieved the target condition.

**Act** corresponds to the adjustments made based on what the follow up reveals. If the countermeasures worked, they become the new standard. If they fell short, the cycle begins again with updated understanding.

Walter Shewhart developed the PDCA cycle at Bell Laboratories in the 1920s, grounding it in the scientific method. W. Edwards Deming brought it to Japan in the 1950s, where it became foundational to Toyota's management approach. Deming preferred "Study" to "Check" (PDSA), emphasizing that the evaluation phase should generate learning, not merely verify compliance. The A3 report embodies this deeper intent. The follow up section is not a checkbox exercise. It is where the organization learns whether its understanding of the problem was correct.

What makes the A3 powerful as a PDCA vehicle is the constraint. The single page forces every phase to be compressed to its essentials. You cannot hide behind verbose analysis. You cannot bury weak logic in dense paragraphs. Everything must be visible, traceable, and concise. The paper is both canvas and discipline.

John Shook captured this insight when he described the A3 as a process for "managing to learn." The PDCA cycle is not merely a problem solving method. It is a learning method. Each rotation through the cycle increases the organization's understanding of its own processes. The A3, by making each rotation visible on a single page, turns that learning into something shareable, reviewable, and teachable.

## How to Fill Out Each Section

### Start with Thinking, Not Writing

David Verble, an experienced A3 coach, offers advice that sounds simple but is rarely followed: "Don't start by writing." The A3 storyboard is the result of thinking, not the process of thinking itself. Filling in boxes does not produce clear thought. Clear thought fills in boxes.

Before touching paper, go to the gemba. Observe. Talk to the people who do the work. Gather data. Form a preliminary understanding. Only then begin recording what you have found.

### Background: Establish the Why

Write two or three sentences that connect the problem to something the organization cares about. A manager reading this section should immediately understand why their attention is warranted. Avoid jargon. Avoid lengthy histories. The background is a doorway, not a room.

**Example:** "Customer complaint rate for our deployment pipeline increased 40% over the past quarter. This directly impacts our service reliability commitments and team velocity."

### Current Condition: Show, Don't Tell

Use visual tools whenever possible. Process maps, data charts, annotated diagrams. The current condition should be based entirely on direct observation and measurable data. If you find yourself writing "I think" or "it seems like," stop and go gather facts.

The discipline here is intellectual honesty. Describe what is, not what you expected or feared. If the data surprises you, good. That means you are learning something.

### Target Condition: Be Specific

Quantify the goal wherever possible. Include a timeframe. The target should be ambitious enough to matter but realistic enough to achieve within one PDCA cycle. If the gap between current and target is enormous, consider breaking the problem into smaller A3s.

### Root Cause Analysis: Drill Deep

Apply the 5 Whys or fishbone diagram to move past symptoms to systemic causes. Watch for the common failure modes of root cause analysis: stopping too early, drifting into blame, forcing a single causal chain when multiple factors contribute.

A useful test: if someone addressed only your identified root cause, would the problem go away? If the answer is uncertain, you may need to dig deeper or branch your analysis.

### Countermeasures: Be Concrete

For each root cause, propose a specific action. Link each countermeasure explicitly to the cause it addresses. If you cannot draw a clear line from countermeasure to root cause, reconsider whether you have the right countermeasure or the right cause.

Use the language of experimentation. These are countermeasures, not final answers. You are proposing a hypothesis: "If we take this action, we expect this result." The follow up will test the hypothesis.

### Implementation Plan: Name Names and Dates

Every task needs an owner and a deadline. Vague plans ("the team will address this") produce vague results. Specific plans ("Keiko will implement the monitoring dashboard by March 15") produce accountability.

### Follow Up: Close the Loop

Define what you will measure, how, and when. Schedule specific review dates. The follow up plan should make it impossible to forget about the A3 after implementation. This is where most A3 efforts fail: not in the analysis, but in the verification.

## A3 as Mentorship

At Toyota, the A3 process is inseparable from the development of people. When a manager assigns someone to own an A3, the manager takes on the role of coach. This coaching follows a specific pattern.

The manager does not tell the A3 owner what the root cause is. The manager does not prescribe countermeasures. Instead, the manager asks questions. "What did you see when you went to the gemba?" "How do you know this is the actual current condition?" "What other causes did you consider?" "Why did you reject them?"

This questioning serves two purposes. First, it improves the quality of the A3 by challenging weak assumptions and uncovered gaps. Second, it teaches the A3 owner how to think. Over time, the owner internalizes the questions. They begin asking themselves what the manager would ask, which means they begin thinking more rigorously without external prompting.

The Lean Enterprise Institute describes this dynamic: "Effective use of the A3 process can facilitate the shift from a debate about who owns what (an authority focused debate) to a dialogue around what is the right thing to do (a responsibility focused conversation)." The A3 earns authority through the quality of its reasoning, not through the hierarchical position of its author. A well constructed A3 from a junior engineer carries more weight than an opinion from a senior executive if the reasoning and evidence are stronger.

This approach echoes the Training Within Industry principles from the 1940s: "If the worker hasn't learned, the teacher hasn't taught." The A3 process makes the teacher accountable for the learner's development, not just for the problem's resolution.

## Practical Applications in Software Teams

Software development already has an iterative culture. Sprints, retrospectives, and continuous integration all reflect PDCA thinking in various forms. The A3 adds something these practices often lack: structured depth on a single problem.

### Incident Response

When a production incident occurs, the A3 format maps naturally onto the investigation. Background: what service was affected and why it matters. Current condition: what the monitoring data and logs show. Root cause analysis: why the failure happened (using 5 Whys or similar). Countermeasures: the specific changes to prevent recurrence. Implementation plan: who will ship the fixes and by when. Follow up: how to verify the fixes hold.

This is more rigorous than a typical postmortem document, which often drifts into narrative without disciplined causal analysis. The A3 format forces the investigation to be systematic.

### Technical Debt Decisions

Teams frequently argue about whether to address technical debt. An A3 can structure the conversation productively. Background: why this technical debt matters to the business. Current condition: what the codebase actually looks like (with metrics, not feelings). Root cause: how the debt accumulated. Countermeasures: specific refactoring or redesign proposals. This transforms an emotional debate into a structured investigation.

### Process Improvement

Retrospectives identify problems. A3s solve them. When a retrospective surfaces a recurring issue ("deployments take too long," "code reviews are a bottleneck"), assigning an A3 owner to investigate creates accountability and structure that a sticky note on a board cannot provide.

### Architecture Decisions

The A3 format can frame architecture decision records with more analytical depth. Rather than simply recording the decision and alternatives, the A3 pushes the team to define the current condition of the system, articulate the specific gap, analyze why the gap exists, and propose countermeasures with explicit follow up criteria.

## Adaptations for AI Agent Operations

This is where the research meets our specific context. Human-AI partnerships and autonomous agent systems face problems that benefit from structured thinking, and the A3 format translates more naturally than you might expect.

### Agent Incident Reports

When an AI agent fails (context compaction destroys a conversation, a tool call produces unexpected results, a decision chain goes wrong), the A3 structure provides a rigorous framework for investigation. The background connects the incident to the system's purpose. The current condition documents what the logs, memory files, and session records actually show. The root cause analysis applies 5 Whys to the failure chain. Countermeasures propose specific changes to prompts, tooling, protocols, or architecture.

Our own compaction incident on January 31, 2026, would have been well served by an A3. The investigation we conducted informally followed A3 logic: we identified the current condition (context window filled during deep conversation), analyzed the root cause chain (no pre-compaction protocol, no handoff practices, hadn't anticipated context limits), and implemented countermeasures (context monitoring, handoff protocols, session management). An explicit A3 would have made this investigation more shareable and reviewable.

### Operational Process Improvement

Agent systems involve many processes that benefit from structured improvement: memory management, tool selection, context prioritization, communication protocols. When one of these processes repeatedly causes friction, an A3 provides a disciplined framework for investigation rather than ad hoc adjustments.

### Mentorship Between Human and Agent

The A3's mentorship dimension applies directly to human-AI collaboration. When a human coaches an AI agent through an A3, the questioning process ("What did you observe?" "How do you know?") calibrates the agent's reasoning and surfaces assumptions that might otherwise go unexamined. The A3 becomes a shared thinking space where both partners contribute to understanding.

### Strategy Alignment

Advanced lean organizations use A3s for strategy deployment, connecting individual problem solving efforts to organizational objectives. In agent operations, this translates to connecting individual improvement efforts (fixing a specific failure mode) to the broader goals of the system (reliable, trustworthy autonomous operation). The A3 format makes this connection explicit.

## Relationship to Other Lean Methods

A3 thinking does not exist in isolation. It is one node in a network of interconnected lean practices, each reinforcing the others.

### 5 Whys

The 5 Whys is the most common analytical tool used inside the root cause analysis section of an A3. The A3 provides the container and the narrative arc; the 5 Whys provides the method for drilling into causes. They are deeply complementary. An A3 without root cause analysis is just a status report. A 5 Whys without the broader context of an A3 can produce narrow conclusions disconnected from action.

### Fishbone Diagrams (Ishikawa)

Where the 5 Whys drills vertically into a single causal chain, the fishbone diagram fans out horizontally across categories of potential causes. Inside an A3, a fishbone diagram often serves as the first step in root cause analysis, mapping the territory before the 5 Whys selects the most promising branch to drill into.

### Value Stream Mapping

Value stream mapping visualizes the end to end flow of a process, distinguishing between value adding steps and waste. A value stream map can reveal where problems originate, which then become subjects for individual A3 investigations. The relationship is hierarchical: value stream mapping identifies improvement opportunities; A3s investigate and address them.

### Standard Work

Standard work documents the current best known method for performing a task. When an A3 investigation produces effective countermeasures, those countermeasures become the new standard work. The A3 is the improvement tool; standard work is how improvements are sustained.

### Kaizen Events

A kaizen event is an intensive, focused improvement effort, typically lasting three to five days. A3s are frequently used to frame kaizen events, providing the investigation that determines where to focus the event's energy and documenting the results afterward.

### Hoshin Kanri (Strategy Deployment)

At the organizational level, hoshin kanri aligns strategic objectives with tactical execution. A3s serve as the mechanism for cascading strategy into specific problem solving efforts. Each level of the organization translates strategic goals into A3s that address the specific obstacles at that level. This creates alignment without centralized command.

### PDCA / PDSA

The A3 is PDCA made visible and tangible. Every A3 is a single iteration of the plan, do, check, act cycle. The relationship is not merely analogical. The A3 was designed to be a physical instantiation of PDCA thinking.

## Strengths

**Forced clarity.** The single page constraint eliminates verbosity and forces the author to distill complex problems to their essentials. If you cannot fit it on one page, you do not understand it well enough.

**Visual communication.** A well constructed A3 communicates the full arc of an investigation in minutes. Anyone can pick it up and follow the logic from background to follow up. This makes organizational learning scalable.

**Structured thinking.** The sections guide the author through a logical sequence that prevents the common failure of jumping from problem to solution. The left side forces you to understand before the right side lets you act.

**Mentorship vehicle.** The coaching dialogue that the A3 generates develops critical thinking skills across the organization. It builds people, not just solutions.

**Consensus building.** Because the A3 is shared and iterated through dialogue, it builds alignment among stakeholders. By the time countermeasures are implemented, everyone involved has had the opportunity to challenge assumptions and contribute to the thinking.

**Accessibility.** No specialized training is required to start using A3s. The format is simple enough for anyone to attempt, yet deep enough to reward years of practice.

**Accountability.** The A3 owner's name on the document creates clear responsibility. The implementation plan and follow up sections prevent the investigation from ending at analysis.

## Limitations

**Deceptive simplicity.** The format looks easy. Fill in seven boxes on a piece of paper. In practice, doing it well requires deep skill in observation, analysis, communication, and facilitation. Organizations that adopt the template without the underlying thinking discipline produce impressive looking documents that change nothing.

**Cultural dependency.** The A3 thrives in cultures of psychological safety, honest dialogue, and genuine curiosity about problems. In organizations where problems are punished, where managers dictate rather than coach, or where appearance matters more than substance, the A3 becomes theater. Toyota spent decades building the culture that makes A3 thinking work. Adopting the tool without the culture produces hollow results.

**Single page limitation.** The constraint is also a limitation. Some problems are genuinely complex enough that a single page cannot contain the investigation without oversimplifying. The A3 format can bias toward problems that fit its format rather than problems that matter most.

**Linear structure.** The left to right, problem to solution flow implies a linearity that complex problems often lack. Real investigations cycle back and forth between understanding and hypothesizing. The A3 captures the final narrative but can obscure the messy reality of the investigation process.

**Documentation overhead.** In fast moving environments, the discipline of completing a full A3 can feel heavy relative to the pace of change. Software teams accustomed to rapid iteration may resist the slower, more deliberate pace that A3 thinking demands. The challenge is finding the right problems for A3 depth versus those that can be addressed with lighter tools.

**Single cause bias (inherited from 5 Whys).** When the root cause analysis section relies heavily on the 5 Whys, it inherits that tool's tendency toward linear, single cause explanations. Combining with fishbone diagrams or the Swiss cheese model can mitigate this, but the A3 format does not inherently demand it.

**Skill ceiling for coaching.** The mentorship dimension only works if the coach is skilled. A manager who has never done rigorous A3 thinking cannot coach others through it effectively. This creates a bootstrapping problem: the tool depends on experienced practitioners who can only be developed through the tool.

## Common Failures

**Treating the A3 as a form to fill out.** The most widespread failure. People complete the sections mechanically without engaging in the underlying thinking. The result is a document that looks right but changes nothing. David Verble's advice is instructive: start with thinking, not writing.

**Skipping the gemba.** Writing the current condition from memory, from secondhand reports, or from assumptions produces an A3 built on sand. Without direct observation, the entire analysis is suspect.

**Jumping to solutions.** The left side of the A3 exists for a reason. People who rush to countermeasures before thoroughly understanding the current condition and root causes waste effort solving the wrong problem.

**No follow up.** An A3 without follow up is a plan without verification. The cycle remains incomplete. Problems recur. Organizational learning does not happen.

**Presenting instead of sharing.** John Shook draws a critical distinction: "Don't present your A3; share your A3." Presenting is a one way broadcast. Sharing is a dialogue. The A3 is meant to invite challenge, questions, and refinement. When it becomes a polished presentation, it has lost its purpose.

**Confusing the template with the thinking.** Organizations sometimes invest heavily in designing the perfect A3 template, debating how many boxes it should have and what each section should be called. This misses the point entirely. The template is scaffolding. The thinking is the building.
