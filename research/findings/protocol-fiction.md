# Protocol Fiction: Research Document

*Compiled 2026-03-22 for issue #14*

## Definition and Origins

**Protocol fiction** is an emerging literary and design genre that uses protocols, systems, and infrastructure as primary narrative devices. Rather than treating technology as backdrop or MacGuffin, protocol fiction makes the *rules themselves* the story: how they shape behavior, constrain possibility, create culture, and determine who thrives or suffers within their logic.

The term does not yet have a single canonical origin point or Wikipedia entry. It sits at the intersection of several established practices:

- **Design fiction** (Bruce Sterling, 2005; Julian Bleecker, 2009): "The deliberate use of diegetic prototypes to suspend disbelief about change." Design fiction creates speculative scenarios narrated through designed artifacts to provoke debate about possible futures.
- **Speculative design** (Anthony Dunne & Fiona Raby): Design proposals that challenge assumptions and provoke debate rather than sell products. Explores "what if?" rather than "what next?"
- **Infrastructure fiction**: A loosely defined practice of storytelling through and about infrastructure (roads, networks, pipes, standards). The term circulated in design/architecture circles in the 2010s, associated with thinkers like Paul Graham Raven, Deb Chachra, and Shannon Mattern.
- **Protocol studies**: The Summer of Protocols program (funded by the Ethereum Foundation, launched 2023) established protocols as a serious interdisciplinary research subject, producing 100+ works across fields from internet architecture to diplomacy to coal mining.

Protocol fiction synthesizes these: it is design fiction where the prototype is a protocol. It is speculative design where the speculation concerns coordination mechanisms. It is infrastructure fiction where the infrastructure is formalized into explicit rules.

## Key Practitioners and Influences

### Direct Lineage

- **Bruce Sterling**: Coined "design fiction" (2005). His novels (*The Zenith Angle*, *Distraction*, *Islands in the Net*) explore how protocols and standards shape geopolitics. Sterling's "design fiction" concept: making speculative futures tangible through artifacts rather than exposition.
- **Julian Bleecker / Near Future Laboratory**: His 2009 essay established design fiction as a practice. The Near Future Laboratory produces speculative catalogs, newspapers, and product manuals from imagined futures, showing how mundane protocols (shopping, commuting, healthcare) might shift.
- **Venkatesh Rao**: Writer behind Ribbonfarm; led the Summer of Protocols program. His writing on "protocol thinking" frames protocols not as mere technical specifications but as fundamental coordination technologies that shape civilization. Key framing: protocols are to institutions what software is to hardware.
- **Alexander Galloway**: *Protocol: How Control Exists after Decentralization* (2004). Foundational academic text arguing that protocols are the defining power structure of networked society, replacing centralized control with distributed but equally powerful governance through standards (TCP/IP, DNS, HTTP).
- **Janna Tay**: Summer of Protocols researcher. Her "A Phenomenology of Protocols" argues protocols encourage particular states of being in participants, making protocol design a moral question, not just an engineering one.

### Literary Antecedents

- **William Gibson**: The Sprawl trilogy doesn't name protocols but lives inside them. Cyberspace in *Neuromancer* is a protocol: rules of engagement, access patterns, ice (Intrusion Countermeasures Electronics) as enforcement mechanism. The street finds its own uses for things, i.e., protocols get subverted.
- **Neal Stephenson**: *Snow Crash* (language as protocol/virus), *Cryptonomicon* (cryptographic protocols as plot), *The Diamond Age* (the Primer as educational protocol, phyles as social protocols). Stephenson may be the most protocol-conscious SF writer.
- **Cory Doctorow**: *Walkaway*, *Little Brother* series. Explicitly about how protocols (surveillance, encryption, consensus) create or destroy freedom. Doctorow writes protocol fiction as political fiction.
- **Ada Palmer**: *Terra Ignota* series. The entire world is governed by explicit protocols: Hive laws, the Sensayer system, transit networks. The plot is driven by what happens when protocols collide, contradict, or get gamed.
- **Ursula K. Le Guin**: *The Dispossessed* is fundamentally about competing social protocols (Anarres vs. Urras). The ansible is a communication protocol that reshapes interstellar politics.
- **Jorge Luis Borges**: "The Library of Babel," "Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius." Systems and rules as the substance of reality. The protocol *is* the world.
- **Kafka**: *The Trial*, *The Castle*. Bureaucratic protocols as existential horror. The protocol is unknowable, inescapable, and indifferent.

### Adjacent Practitioners

- **Anthony Dunne & Fiona Raby**: *Speculative Everything* (2013). The theoretical backbone of speculative design. Their work frames design as a tool for imagining alternative presents and futures.
- **Tim Maughan**: *Infinite Detail* (2019). A novel about what happens when the internet is destroyed. Infrastructure fiction par excellence.
- **Ingrid Burrington**: *Networks of New York* (2016). Makes invisible infrastructure visible. Documents the physical layer of protocols embedded in urban space.
- **Shannon Mattern**: *Code and Clay, Data and Dirt* (2017). Media archaeology of urban infrastructure. Protocols as sedimented layers of city life.
- **Chenoe Hart**: Summer of Protocols researcher exploring "addressable space," how spatial addressing protocols shape built environments.

## How Protocols Become Narrative Devices

### The Protocol as Character

Protocols can function as characters in fiction: they have agency (they constrain and enable), they have personality (TCP is reliable and slow; UDP is fast and reckless), and they can be antagonists (DRM, surveillance systems) or allies (encryption, mesh networks). The most compelling protocol fiction treats the protocol not as setting but as a force with its own logic and momentum.

### The Protocol as World

In the strongest protocol fiction, the protocol *is* the world. Borges understood this: the Library of Babel is nothing but its protocol (hexagonal rooms, fixed shelves, every permutation of characters). The world-building is the rule-building. The fiction emerges from following the rules to their logical conclusions.

This is distinct from "worldbuilding" in the Tolkien tradition, which builds worlds through history, geography, and culture. Protocol fiction builds worlds through *rules of interaction*: what is permitted, what is forbidden, what is possible, what is enforced, what happens at the edges.

### The Protocol as Plot

Plots in protocol fiction tend to emerge from:

1. **Protocol collision**: Two incompatible systems meet (Le Guin's *The Dispossessed*, Palmer's *Terra Ignota*)
2. **Protocol failure**: A system breaks down, revealing what it was holding together (Maughan's *Infinite Detail*)
3. **Protocol subversion**: Characters exploit the gap between protocol intent and protocol implementation (Gibson's hackers, Doctorow's cypherpunks)
4. **Protocol discovery**: Characters realize they are inside a protocol they didn't know existed (Kafka, certain Borges stories)
5. **Protocol design**: Characters deliberately create new protocols and face consequences (Stephenson's *Cryptonomicon*, blockchain fiction)

### The Mundane Future

Design fiction, per the Near Future Laboratory, is about "the future mundane": not grand space opera but the everyday experience of living inside new systems. Protocol fiction inherits this. The most powerful protocol fiction isn't about dramatic protocol failures; it's about what it *feels like* to live inside a protocol every day. The phenomenological dimension (per Janna Tay): protocols create states of being.

## Relationship to Adjacent Genres

| Genre | Focus | Protocol Fiction Overlap |
|-------|-------|------------------------|
| **Design fiction** | Diegetic prototypes, speculative artifacts | Protocol fiction prototypes *rules* rather than *objects* |
| **Speculative design** | Alternative presents, provocative proposals | Protocol fiction speculates about coordination mechanisms |
| **Science fiction** | Technology, society, futures | Protocol fiction foregrounds the invisible systems SF often treats as backdrop |
| **Infrastructure fiction** | Physical/digital infrastructure | Protocol fiction formalizes infrastructure into explicit rules |
| **Systems fiction** | Complex systems, emergence | Protocol fiction focuses on the *designed* rules within systems |
| **Cyberpunk** | Technology, power, subversion | Protocol fiction is cyberpunk's analytical descendant: less aesthetic, more structural |
| **Kafka-esque / bureaucratic fiction** | Institutional power, absurdity | Protocol fiction extends this to computational and networked protocols |

## Practical Insights for Writing Protocol Fiction

### 1. Start with the Rule, Not the World
Traditional worldbuilding asks "what is this place?" Protocol fiction asks "what are the rules here?" The world emerges from the rules. Define the protocol first: what does it permit, forbid, incentivize, and punish?

### 2. Show the Protocol Through Friction
Protocols become visible when they create friction: waiting, queuing, authenticating, failing, retrying. The most human moments in protocol fiction come from the gap between what the protocol demands and what the person needs.

### 3. Make the Invisible Visible
Most protocols are invisible until they break. Protocol fiction's job is to make them visible *before* they break, to show the water the fish swims in. Describe the protocol as a character might experience it, not as an engineer might spec it.

### 4. Explore the Edges
The most interesting territory in any protocol is the boundary: what happens when you reach the protocol's limits? Who gets excluded? What behavior does it incentivize that its designers didn't intend? Edge cases are where the story lives.

### 5. Layer Protocols
Real life is a stack of overlapping protocols (social, technical, legal, biological). The richest protocol fiction layers multiple protocols and explores their interactions, conflicts, and emergent behaviors.

### 6. The Phenomenological Dimension
Don't just describe what the protocol *does*; describe what it *feels like* to be inside it. Janna Tay's insight: protocols create states of being. A TSA security line is not just a process; it is an experience of submission, anxiety, performance, and relief.

### 7. Transmissions as Form
The dispatch/transmission/log format (which Ξ2T uses) is native to protocol fiction. Protocols generate artifacts: logs, receipts, status messages, error codes. Writing *as* protocol output (rather than about it) collapses the distance between form and content.
