### Intro

"The street finds its own uses for things." That wasn't me. That line you just heard, indistinguishable from my actual voice, was generated by AI, you had no idea. Wake up. You already live in a cyberpunk world. Your phone tracks everything that you do. Your boss monitors if you're active, and somehow your computer serves you ads for products that you were just thinking about. And that's just the tip of the iceberg. Those headlines, they aren't from the latest episode of _Black Mirror_ either. This is the news. Today, tomorrow. Right now.

But before the Metaverse was born, Neal Stephenson coined the term in _Snow Crash_. Before the Internet took over every aspect of our lives, William Gibson imagined cyberspace, a shared digital world operating 24/7. Before the Walled City of Kowloon was rubble, _Metropolis_ and _Dredd_ showed us vertical cities crushing the poor. The loop of life imitating art and art imitating life has never felt more obvious than with cyberpunk.

### What is cyberpunk? Where did it come from?

Cyberpunk is a subgenre, or, more accurately, a **mode** of science fiction. It heavily leans to the soft side of sci-fi, meaning it uses technology as a propellant to explore character relationships, psychology, philosophy, and societal dynamics. It features countercultural antiheroes trapped in dehumanized high-tech dystopias. These are worlds where corporations wear the crowns, megacities and urban sprawls house the world's population, and the fusion of flesh and machine is the new normal.

You've heard this before, but summed up best: **cyberpunk is high tech and low life.** Technology has never been more widespread and accessible. Societal decay has never been higher. Cyberpunk is a socioeconomic critique and a counter-argument to rapid technological progress. It boldly claims that technology that outpaces our capacity to nurture our ethics, our morality, and to understand our human nature means regression, not progression, and that's especially true when this technology aggregates in the hands of a few.

### Etymology: Cyber

There are even more clues if we break down the term **cyber**. It originates from Norbert Wiener's **cybernetics**, the study of self-regulating feedback loops between systems. These insights set a theoretical foundation for everything from electronic engineering and computing to automation, telecommunications, and neuroscience. In the context of cyberpunk, this usually means man and machine, flesh and metal: self-regulating systems that blur the boundary between human and artificial. The root of cybernetics is the best example to visualize this: _kybernētēs_, Greek for "steersman." Moving a ship's rudder creates a feedback loop. Adjust the angle, adjust the direction.

### Etymology: Punk

You might think **punk** is more self-explanatory, but punk isn't just about mohawks and mosh pits. Punk is a mentality first and foremost. It's counter-authoritarian, anti-establishment, and a refusal to submit. It's saying no when the system says comply. It's an outright rejection of fatalism, or, for others, accepting it but moving forward anyway. It's a do-it-yourself attitude: modifying your tech, repurposing and weaponizing scraps, and puncturing through the established order in any way that you can.

That intro sentence, "The street finds its own uses for things", that's punk. Without punk, you likely just have dystopian sci-fi with cool gadgets. With it, you have critique, satire, and edge: gonzo journalism, psychotic rebels, amphetamine-addicted hackers, and they are all very, very pissed off. Cyberpunk: it's a bit of an oxymoron if you think about it.

### Origins: Who blended these ideas?

The actual term was created in the early 1980s by **Bruce Bethke**, who used it as the title for his short story. Bethke was actively trying to invent a new term that fused the juxtaposition of punk attitudes and high technology. His motivation was just to create a snappy one-word title that editors could remember. But the term was actually **popularized by Gardner Dozois**, using it in his December article "Science Fiction in the 80s", except he wasn't using it to describe Bethke. He was mainly using it to describe William Gibson and other writers in the 80s cyberpunk movement. Had Dozois not labeled them cyberpunks, we might be calling this genre something entirely different.

And therein lies the rocket fuel for the operating system of the genre: **Neuromancer**, the quintessential cyberpunk novel, published in 1984, a blend of noir, grit, substance-abusing hackers, street samurai, and corporate interests. By Bethke's own admission, he credits _Neuromancer_ as the real defining work of the movement. And for good reason.

So if _Neuromancer_ is the operating system of the genre, what's the rocket fuel for the **UI** of the genre, the look? That's **Blade Runner**, directed by Ridley Scott and featuring Harrison Ford, released in 1982. It's the quintessential hard-boiled cyber-noir, adapted from Philip K. Dick's _Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?_ And while this was the movement in the West, Eastern cyberpunk was forming its own lineage, making its biggest impact in manga and anime.

**Bruce invented the word. Dozois popularized it. Gibson built the world. Ridley Scott formed the cinematic language.** Make no mistake, this was all on the shoulders of influences, works, and movements, mainly in the West and in Europe. But we'll get into that in the future.

### Eight core ingredients

Despite what you might hear, the genre does not have a definitive balance and doesn't fit into a neat, tidy box. But there are **eight core ingredients** in the formula of cyberpunk. Not every work has to include all eight, and they can be seasoned to taste with additional spices, some fairly critical to the overall dish (akin to sugar and salt) and some a little more niche.

---

#### Ingredient 1: Corporate rule

In cyberpunk, corporations don't just sell you products. They function as governments, militaries, and infrastructure all rolled into one. They write and rewrite the rules, whether explicitly or implicitly, and enforce them with private armies and lucrative contracts. They own the flow of information, control the media, and quietly pull the strings. Cyberpunk stories use corporations to ask potent questions: What happens when profit has more power than policy? When unelected boards of corporate execs have more power than a president or parliament? Every part of your life is downstream of corporate incentive, and that water is usually tainted.

**Examples:**

- **Tyrell Corporation** (_Blade Runner_). Building artificial life and replicants; effectively playing God. They answer to nobody.
- **Tessier-Ashpool** (_Neuromancer_). Enigmatic orbital family empire with a god-tier AI, totally insulated and operating through obscurity.
- **Arasaka**. Toes the line between explicit and implicit: megacorp with its own army, black ops, political puppets, and a lot of shady stuff behind the scenes.

**Real-world parallel:** Apple, Google, Meta, Amazon, Tencent, Nvidia, companies with GDP-sized valuations, private data empires, and more direct influence on your daily routine than your local government.

---

#### Ingredient 2: Tech-driven inequality

In a cyberpunk dystopia, advanced technology is not a rising sea that raises all ships. It doesn't increase quality of life for the marginalized nor flatten class divides. It's used as another wedge: the wealthy use the best tech to extend health, lifespan, and mass control; the poor are kept siloed on the streets through products that range from pacifying to predatory. Black-market upgrades and the DIY underground are really no better. Traditional utopian sci-fi says technology is an equalizer. Cyberpunk flips that: _What if the cable you're jacking into the back of your skull is actually just a chain?_ You're effectively a rabid dog with a stake in the ground. The issue is not the tech itself, it's when the tech grows without the guardrails of humanity.

**Examples:**

- **Edgerunners**. The cost of upward mobility is only achievable through reckless augmentation; Martinez pushes too far and the tech eats him alive.
- **Blade Runner**. Replicants as engineered slaves with a four-year lifespan; megacorps creating disposable labor.

---

#### Ingredient 3: Surveillance and data extraction

In many genre works, everything is watched and logged: your movements, biometrics, relationships, thought patterns. They're turned into data and weaponized as products. Privacy is a grayed-out opt-in box. You're not a person, you're data with purchasing power.

**Manifestations:**

- **Construct ROMs** (_Neuromancer_). Dead hackers recycled into software tools; little to no agency.
- **Netwatch and the Turing Police**. Monitoring the net, policing the flow of information; judge, jury, and executioner over what intelligence is allowed to exist.
- **Data economies**. Information as leverage; new methods of extraction creating whole new economies, legit or illicit.

You're a glorified shark with about 300 trackers on your fins. You're fully mapped out.

---

#### Ingredient 4: Identity and transhumanism

In many genre works, bodies become modular, memories editable, personalities files. Every bodily function maps to a computer function, the self-regulating feedback loop. This is where cyberpunk and the soft side of sci-fi shine. Cyberpunk asks: _At what point do you stop being you?_ It doesn't just explore what tech can do; it asks what we lose when we use it.

**Examples:**

- **Ghost in the Shell** (Puppet Master). An intelligence born from data claiming personhood; legal and philosophical crisis. Are artificial intelligences different from human intelligences? Does this intelligence have a right to life?
- **Blade Runner** (Deckard's ambiguity). Is Deckard human? At the end of the day, does it matter?

**Real-world parallels:** Brain-computer interfaces, deepfake identities, AI voice clones (like the intro of the video), algorithms that know you better than your family. Who would you tell your deepest secrets to, your family or Google?

---

#### Ingredient 5: Cyberspace

In cyberpunk works, network simulations and virtual spaces aren't just tools, they're parallel realities where the power balance can shift. Cyberspace is an ecosystem of its own, in many ways functionally similar to outer space in terms of mystery and awe. Whether it's called the Net or the Matrix, it's as legit a place as meatspace, and often treated as more consequential than the physical streets. The power lies where information flows. If you control the network, you control reality. Cyberspace is the most impactful, and equally deadly, landscape for punks trying to make that impact, and it's used to show the inability to categorize reality.

From _Neuromancer_:

> A hallucinatory dataverse where console cowboys jack in. Experience daily by billions of legitimate operators in every nation. By children being taught mathematical concepts. A graphic representation of data abstracted from the banks of every computer in the human system. Unthinkable complexity. Lines of light ranged in the non-space of the mind. Clusters and constellations of data like city lights receding.

You see this in _The Matrix_ (what is real?) and differently in the old Net and the Black Wall in _Cyberpunk 2077_. But hackers alone don't make something cyberpunk, you need systemic critique, the dystopia, and punk resistance. Cyberspace is the canvas for rebellion, transhumanism, the surreality of reality, and spiritual connotations.

**Real-world parallels:** VR, NFTs, web3, always-online social media, YouTube. Has your smartphone become one with you? Do you control the feedback system, or does it control you?

---

#### Ingredient 6: Ecological collapse

In megacities, the natural world is either dead, dying, or inaccessible. Humanity retreats into massive vertical hyper-densities; nature is a luxury product or only visible in simulation. The city itself is the antagonist, brutal skyscrapers as prison bars, or a reminder not to rock the boat. Megacities introduce the **vertical class system**: poverty on the jungle floor, wealth in the penthouses, off-world colonies for the ultra-elite. Social stratification as literal vertical stratification.

**Examples:** Peach Trees in _Dredd_, Neo Tokyo in _Akira_, and most other cyberpunk stories. Megacities usually exist because of ecological collapse, Earth pillaged, point of no return. You physically can't escape; if you do, you find an irradiated wasteland.

---

#### Ingredient 7: Punk resistance and noir DNA

The street does fight back, but poorly, consistently, and often self-destructively. Marginalized street dwellers, data thieves, mercs, and misfits fight or just survive against systems too big to topple. Facing the system on its terms is pointless if not suicide, whack-a-mole with a toothpick against a 20-headed hydra. Cyberpunk also has deep roots in **film noir** and borrows heavily from it in neon-noir depictions. Punk is the serrated teeth, the edge; noir gives heart-wrenching tragedy, bleeding out on a sidewalk. When works are more punk-centric, they dangle hope that the DIY street-resistance mind virus might spread. When more noir, it's hard-boiled detectives uncovering conspiracies they can't stop. Victories are usually hollow.

**Examples:** Biker gangs in _Akira_; the mercenary who refuses to be owned (Molly Millions in _Neuromancer_); Elliot in _Mr. Robot_ (wouldn't be caught dead in a riot, but in front of a computer he can take the world down); Replicants in _Blade Runner_, punk themes in their desire to break free from enslavement; for Deckard, rigid and anything but punk, noir is the not-so-secret ingredient.

---

#### Ingredient 8: Escapism and substance use

In cyberpunk worlds, drugs, chemical and technological, are everywhere: escapism for those trapped at the bottom, performance enhancers for corporate execs and military agents, pacification tools. These realities are unbearable, so substances can be as omnipresent as tech or tied to it.

- **Chemical:** Substance addiction; gangs in _Akira_ popping pills.
- **Technological escape:** Braindance, SimStim, the squid headset.
- **Performance:** Smart drugs, combat stims, time-slowing inhalers (_Dredd_).
- **Pacification:** e.g. _Brave New World_ with Soma.

Our real world has addictions too.
