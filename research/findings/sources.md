# Sources Bibliography: UX Research Scenarios

---

## Nielsen Norman Group (NNG)

### 1. "Turn User Goals into Task Scenarios for Usability Testing"
- **Author**: Hoa Loranger
- **URL**: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/task-scenarios-usability-testing/
- **Published**: 2014 (updated)
- **Why it matters**: The foundational NNG article on task scenario writing. Establishes the three core principles (realistic, actionable, non-leading) that every other source references. Includes concrete before/after examples.

### 2. "Write Better Qualitative Usability Tasks: Top 10 Mistakes to Avoid"
- **Author**: Kathryn Whitenton
- **URL**: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/better-usability-tasks/
- **Published**: 2017
- **Why it matters**: The most comprehensive catalog of scenario anti-patterns from any single source. Each mistake includes a concrete example with a corrected version. Essential reading for anyone writing scenarios.

### 3. "From Research Goals to Usability-Testing Scenarios: A 7-Step Method"
- **Author**: Page Laubheimer
- **URL**: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-research-goals-to-scenarios/
- **Published**: 2020
- **Why it matters**: Provides the complete upstream process for scenario creation, from stakeholder concerns through problem statements to finished scenarios. The only source that systematically connects business goals to scenario writing.

### 4. "Authentic Behavior in User Testing"
- **Author**: Jakob Nielsen
- **URL**: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/authentic-behavior-in-user-testing/
- **Published**: 2015 (updated)
- **Why it matters**: Explains WHY scenarios work psychologically (suspension of disbelief, engagement). Provides techniques for rescuing sessions when scenarios fail to produce authentic behavior, including adding constraints and accountability.

### 5. "Usability Testing 101"
- **Author**: Kate Moran
- **URL**: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/usability-testing-101/
- **Published**: 2019
- **Why it matters**: Contextualizes scenarios within the broader usability testing methodology. Defines the relationship between facilitator, tasks, and participants. Good examples of real usability test scenarios.

### 6. "Avoid Leading Questions to Get Better Insights from Participants"
- **Author**: Kathryn Whitenton
- **URL**: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/leading-questions/
- **Published**: 2020
- **Why it matters**: Directly relevant to scenario writing because leading questions and leading scenarios share the same root problem: injecting researcher bias into the participant's behavior. Provides the framework for identifying priming effects in both questions and task phrasing.

## Books (Practitioner Canon)

### 7. "About Face: The Essentials of Interaction Design" (4th Edition)
- **Authors**: Alan Cooper, Robert Reimann, David Cronin, Christopher Noessel
- **Publisher**: Wiley, 2014
- **Why it matters**: Definitive source on goal-directed design scenarios. Introduces the taxonomy of context scenarios, key path scenarios, and validation scenarios. The persona-scenario pair is Cooper's foundational design method.

### 8. "Designing for the Digital Age: How to Create Human-Centered Products and Services"
- **Author**: Kim Goodwin
- **Publisher**: Wiley, 2009
- **Why it matters**: The most thorough practitioner treatment of scenarios in design. Goodwin (former VP of Design at Cooper) adds environmental, emotional, and social context layers to Cooper's scenario framework. 700+ pages of deeply researched methodology.

### 9. "Making Use: Scenario-Based Design of Human-Computer Interactions"
- **Author**: John M. Carroll
- **Publisher**: MIT Press, 2000
- **Why it matters**: The academic foundation for all scenario-based design. Carroll originated the methodology at Penn State in the 1990s. This book provides the theoretical framework that Cooper, Goodwin, and others built upon. Cited thousands of times in HCI literature.

### 10. "Scenario-Based Design: Envisioning Work and Technology in System Development"
- **Editor**: John M. Carroll
- **Publisher**: Wiley, 1995
- **Why it matters**: The earlier edited volume that collected foundational papers on scenario-based approaches from multiple HCI researchers. Establishes scenarios as a legitimate design methodology in academic HCI.

### 11. "Rocket Surgery Made Easy: The Do-It-Yourself Guide to Finding and Fixing Usability Problems"
- **Author**: Steve Krug
- **Publisher**: New Riders, 2010
- **Why it matters**: The most accessible guide to practical usability testing, including scenario writing. Krug's emphasis on keeping scenarios simple and his "tell me back" pilot testing technique are widely adopted. Written for non-specialists who need to run usability tests.

### 12. "Don't Make Me Think, Revisited"
- **Author**: Steve Krug
- **Publisher**: New Riders, 2014 (3rd edition)
- **Why it matters**: While focused more broadly on usability, contains practical guidance on writing test tasks that complements "Rocket Surgery Made Easy." The chapter on usability testing includes scenario examples.

### 13. "The User Experience Team of One: A Research and Design Survival Guide"
- **Author**: Leah Buley
- **Publisher**: Rosenfeld Media, 2013
- **Why it matters**: Provides lean, resource-constrained approaches to scenario creation. Buley's proto-persona to scenario workflow is the most practical approach for solo practitioners or small teams without dedicated researchers.

### 14. "Mental Models: Aligning Design Strategy with Human Behavior"
- **Author**: Indi Young
- **Publisher**: Rosenfeld Media, 2008
- **Why it matters**: Young's mental model diagrams provide a rigorous method for understanding how users think about a problem space, which directly informs scenario writing. Her work emphasizes understanding user intent and thinking patterns before designing solutions.

### 15. "Practical Empathy: For Collaboration and Creativity in Your Work"
- **Author**: Indi Young
- **Publisher**: Rosenfeld Media, 2015
- **Why it matters**: Builds on mental models with techniques for understanding user motivations, which is the essential input for writing scenarios that reflect real behavior rather than assumed behavior.

## Academic Sources

### 16. "Five Reasons for Scenario-Based Design"
- **Author**: John M. Carroll
- **Published**: 1999, Proceedings of the 32nd Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences
- **Why it matters**: Carroll's concise articulation of why scenarios work as design tools: they are concrete, flexible, grounded, and encourage stakeholder participation. The theoretical justification for using scenarios at all.

### 17. "Usability Problem Description and the Evaluator Effect in Usability Testing"
- **Authors**: Molich, Ede, Halkjaer, Jensen
- **Published**: 2004, Interacting with Computers
- **Why it matters**: Demonstrates how different evaluators using the same scenarios find different problems, which has implications for scenario design: scenarios must be specific enough to produce comparable results across facilitators.

## Online Practitioner Resources

### 18. Jeff Sauro / MeasuringU
- **URL**: https://measuringu.com/
- **Why it matters**: Sauro's quantitative approach to usability metrics includes guidance on writing task scenarios that produce measurable outcomes (task success rates, time on task, error rates). His work bridges qualitative scenario writing with quantitative measurement.
- **Key publication**: "A Practical Guide to the System Usability Scale" (included scenario writing guidance)
- **Note**: Several specific MeasuringU articles on scenarios have been restructured or moved; the site's search function is the best way to access current content.

### 19. Jared Spool / UIE (now Center Centre)
- **URL**: https://articles.uie.com/
- **Why it matters**: Spool's articles on usability testing methodology include practical scenario guidance, particularly around testing information architecture. His concept of "activity-focused design" aligns with scenario-based approaches.

### 20. IDEO / d.school Design Thinking Resources
- **URL**: https://dschool.stanford.edu/resources
- **Why it matters**: The d.school's design thinking toolkit includes scenario-based methods, particularly in the "prototype" and "test" phases. Their emphasis on empathy-driven design directly informs how scenarios should be grounded in user research rather than assumptions.

## Notes on Source Quality

All sources listed here meet at least one of these criteria:
- Published by a recognized authority in UX research methodology (NNG, Cooper, Rosenfeld Media)
- Authored by a practitioner with 15+ years of documented UX research experience
- Published in a peer-reviewed HCI venue
- Cited extensively by other authoritative sources in the field

Sources that were considered but excluded:
- Generic UX blog posts without cited research or documented practitioner experience
- Content marketing articles from UX tool vendors
- Aggregation sites that summarize other sources without adding original insight
