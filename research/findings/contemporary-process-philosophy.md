## Introduction: The Process Turn

Process philosophy represents one of the most vital and under-recognized currents in contemporary metaphysics. While the classical foundations of process thought -- laid by Alfred North Whitehead, Henri Bergson, and Charles Sanders Peirce -- are increasingly acknowledged in the history of philosophy, the contemporary flowering of process philosophy across analytical, continental, and interdisciplinary domains remains largely unmapped within mainstream philosophical discourse.

The central thesis of process philosophy can be stated simply and then complicated: reality is fundamentally processual rather than substantial. Where substance metaphysics, from the pre-Socratics through Aristotle to much of contemporary analytic ontology beginning with Quine's "On What There Is," takes static entities -- substances, objects, individuals -- as the primary ontological units, process philosophy insists that being is dynamic, that becoming is primary, and that what we experience as stable entities are themselves emergent patterns of ongoing processes.

Johanna Seibt's Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on process philosophy frames this as a paradigm shift. As Seibt notes, substance metaphysics "proceeds from the intuition -- first formulated by the pre-Socratic Greek philosopher Parmenides -- that being should be thought of as simple, hence as internally undifferentiated and unchangeable." Process philosophers, by contrast, take ubiquitous dynamicity as metaphysically primary, not as a secondary feature of fundamentally static things.

The contemporary process turn is remarkable for its breadth. It spans continental thinkers like Deleuze and Stengers who radicalize process thought into a full critique of representational thinking; analytic philosophers like Nicholas Rescher who reformulate process ontology in the idiom of rigorous logical analysis; media theorists like Katherine Hayles who deploy process frameworks for understanding posthuman subjectivity and computational systems; and interdisciplinary scientists like Ilya Prigogine whose work on dissipative structures provides empirical grounding for a process metaphysics that is no longer merely speculative.

Simultaneously, process philosophy has entered into productive and adversarial dialogue with other contemporary metaphysical movements -- Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO), speculative realism, new materialism -- each of which negotiates its own relationship to the primacy of process. Whether through the flat ontology of Graham Harman, the agential realism of Karen Barad, or the vital materialism of Jane Bennett, the fundamental question of whether reality is best understood through substances or processes remains a live and deeply contested philosophical question.

This document surveys the contemporary landscape of process philosophy, examining its key figures, its intersections with science and technology, and its role in current metaphysical debates.

## 1. Gilles Deleuze: Difference, Becoming, and Anti-Identitarian Metaphysics

Gilles Deleuze (1925-1995) stands as arguably the most influential process philosopher of the second half of the twentieth century. His philosophy represents a radical departure from the "identitarian" tradition -- the lineage from Parmenides through Aristotle, Aquinas, Descartes, Kant, and Hegel that privileges identity, sameness, and representation over difference, becoming, and production.

### 1.1 Difference-in-Itself: The Ontological Primacy of Difference

The cornerstone of Deleuze's process ontology is his reconception of difference. In the history of Western metaphysics, difference has consistently been subordinated to identity. From Aristotle's principle of non-contradiction to Kant's transcendental unity of apperception to Hegel's dialectical synthesis, difference has been understood only as a derivative phenomenon -- the difference *between* self-identical substances, the difference *between* the concept and the object, the difference that is ultimately sublated or overcome in a higher unity.

In *Difference and Repetition* (1968), Deleuze attempts to think difference "in itself" -- difference that is not the difference *between* two identities but is itself productive, generative, and ontologically primary. This is the radical core of Deleuzean process metaphysics: difference does not presuppose identity; identity is a product of differential processes. As the SEP entry notes, Deleuze "attaches the critical axiom that the ground cannot resemble that which it grounds" -- the transcendental field must be impersonal, pre-individual, and purely differential, while identities of subject and object are products of processes that resolve or actualize this differential field.

Deleuze's conception of being as difference-in-itself carries direct processual implications. If being is difference, then being is inherently dynamic, inherently productive, inherently processual. Difference is not a relation between static terms; it is the engine that produces terms. This makes Deleuze's metaphysics a philosophy of becoming: what *is*, is what it does, what it produces, what it differentiates.

### 1.2 The Virtual and the Actual: Beyond Possible/Real

One of Deleuze's most consequential philosophical moves -- with profound implications for process thought -- is his distinction between the virtual and the actual, which replaces the traditional possible/real distinction. Drawing heavily on Bergson's *Creative Evolution*, Deleuze argues that the possible/real distinction relies on a "false problem" that confuses the more with the less.

When we think of the possible as somehow "pre-existing" the real, we first conceive of the real, then subtract existence from it, and project this image back into the past as the "possible." We then say the possible has been "realized." But this, Deleuze argues, is a backwards illusion. The virtual, by contrast, is not a diminished version of the real but a fully real domain of differential relations, singularities, and multiplicities that generates the actual through a process of differentiation (not "realization").

The virtual is the condition of genesis, not the condition of possibility. It has "no more possibility than it has reality" -- it is a real field of differential forces that actualizes itself in the production of actual entities. This reconception is fundamentally processual: the virtual is not a storehouse of pre-formed possibilities waiting to be actualized but a generative field in which actualization is an ongoing, creative, unpredictable process. The actualization of the virtual is a genuinely productive process -- what Deleuze calls "differentiation" (in the virtual) and "differenciation" (in the actual) -- that produces novel, irreducible realities.

### 1.3 Multiplicity: Replacing Substance with Process

Deleuze adopts the concept of "multiplicity" from Bernhard Riemann (via Bergson) as the key category for his differential ontology. A multiplicity is not a "multiple" attribute of some unitary thing -- not "many" versus "one" -- but a substantive in its own right: the multiple *itself*. This is a crucial grammatical and metaphysical shift.

As the SEP notes, "the multiple as predicate generates a set of philosophical problems under the rubric of 'the one and the many.' With multiplicity, or the multiple as substantive, the question of the relation between the predicates one/multiple is replaced by the question of distinguishing types of multiplicities." A multiplicity is defined by its virtual structure -- by the differential relations among its elements -- rather than by the properties of its constituents. It is intrinsically processual: a multiplicity is defined by how it changes, how it is transformed, how it enters into relations.

### 1.4 Rhizome versus Arborescence: Anti-Hierarchical Process

In *A Thousand Plateaus* (1980), written with Félix Guattari, Deleuze develops the concept of the rhizome as an alternative to "arborescent" (tree-like) models of thought. The rhizome is a non-hierarchical, decentered network of connections -- the model of grass, of underground stems, of the map rather than the tracing.

The arborescent model is the model of substance metaphysics: a single trunk (origin, foundation, root) from which branches (categories, derivations) emerge in a linear, hierarchical fashion. The rhizome, by contrast, operates by principles of connection, heterogeneity, multiplicity, and asignment. Any point of a rhizome can and must be connected to any other. This is process metaphysics as a philosophy of relationality -- not relations between substances, but relations as the very constitution of what exists.

The rhizomatic model has profound implications for understanding both thought and reality. It suggests that the proper ontology for a world of processes is not an ontology of discrete substances but an ontology of networks, connections, flows, and becomings.

### 1.5 Becoming: Not Becoming-Something but Becoming-Itself

Perhaps the most radical Deleuzean concept for process philosophy is "becoming." Deleuze distinguishes sharply between "being" in the traditional sense (fixed, self-identical) and "becoming" (dynamic, relational, creative). But becoming, for Deleuze, is not the process *by which* one substance transforms into another. It is itself the fundamental reality.

The Deleuze-Guattari concept of "becoming-animal," "becoming-woman," "becoming-imperceptible" should not be understood as metaphorical or psychological. They are ontological claims about the nature of reality. To "become-animal" is not to imitate or identify with an animal; it is to enter into a zone of proximity or indiscernibility where the distinction between organism and environment, subject and object, breaks down. This is process as the very texture of reality -- not the movement of things through space but the ongoing production and transformation of differences.

### 1.6 The Body without Organs: Anti-Organizational Process

The "body without organs" (BwO) is one of Deleuze and Guattari's most challenging and most important concepts for understanding process ontology. The BwO is not a biological body stripped of its organs; it is a field of intensive forces and flows that resists any fixed organization into functional, stratified systems.

The organism, for Deleuze-Guattari, is a "stratification" of the BwO -- an organization of flows into stable, functional units. But stratification is always temporary, always subject to de-stratification. The BwO is the immanent plane of consistency on which processes unfold before and beneath their organization into discrete, functional entities. It is the pure processuality that underlies and exceeds any particular organization.

### 1.7 The Plane of Immanence

The plane of immanence is Deleuze's most abstract and most fundamental ontological concept. It is the pre-conceptual, pre-philosophical field from which all concepts emerge and to which they refer. It is not a concept itself but the "image of thought" that makes concepts possible. As a process-philosophical category, the plane of immanence represents the absolute immanence of all processes -- the claim that there is no transcendent realm outside the field of becoming from which being could be judged or organized.

### 1.8 Deleuze as Anti-Identitarian Radical Process Metaphysics

Summing up Deleuze's contribution to process philosophy: his entire philosophical project can be read as an extended argument against the primacy of identity. From his early critique of Kant's transcendental subject to his mature work with Guattari on capitalism and schizophrenia, Deleuze consistently insists that identity is a secondary effect of primary differential processes.

This makes Deleuze's metaphysics a more radical form of process philosophy even than Whitehead's. Where Whitehead's actual occasions are still individual entities (however processually conceived), Deleuze's ontology is one of pure differential processes, of multiplicity, of becoming. The individual thing -- the "subject," the "object," the "organism" -- is not the starting point but the result, the effect, the sedimentation of processes.

Deleuze's influence on subsequent process thought is immense. His collaboration with Félix Guattari extended these ideas into political philosophy, psychoanalysis, and cultural theory. His reading of Bergson revitalized Bergsonian process philosophy for a new generation. His concepts -- deterritorialization, rhizome, assemblage, smooth/striated space, lines of flight -- have become the vocabulary of contemporary process thinking across disciplines.

## 2. Isabelle Stengers: Cosmopolitics and the Ecology of Practices

Isabelle Stengers (b. 1949) is perhaps the most important contemporary figure extending process philosophy into science studies, political ecology, and what she calls "cosmopolitics." A Belgian philosopher of science, Stengers was trained in chemistry and studied under Ilya Prigogine (Nobel laureate for work on dissipative structures), and has become the foremost contemporary interpreter and developer of Whitehead's process philosophy.

### 2.1 Whitehead's Influence on Stengers

Stengers' engagement with Whitehead is deep and sustained. In her book *Thinking with Whitehead: A Free and Wild Creation of Concepts* (2011, English translation 2014), Stengers takes Whitehead not merely as a historical figure to be interpreted but as a living resource for thinking through the crises of the contemporary world.

Stengers is drawn to Whitehead's insistence on the reality of experience all the way down -- not just human experience, but the "feeling," "prehension," and "satisfaction" of every actual occasion. This is not anthropomorphism but a refusal to accept the bifurcation of nature into primary and secondary qualities, into an objective world of dead matter and a subjective world of human meaning. For Stengers, this is the central insight of process philosophy: that the division between "nature" and "experience" is itself a product of a particular (and deeply problematic) set of modern scientific and philosophical practices.

Stengers also emphasizes Whitehead's concept of God -- not as a transcendent creator but as the "principle of limitation" or "principle of concretion" that grounds novelty and order in the process of actualization. In Stengers' reading, this is a genuinely cosmological function: God is what makes novelty possible without making it predetermined. God is the lure, the invitation, the "call" that the future makes on the present.

### 2.2 Cosmopolitics

Stengers' concept of "cosmopolitics" is her most significant theoretical innovation and her most direct extension of process thought into the political domain. The term appears in the introduction to *Cosmopolitiques* (1996-99), a nine-volume collaborative project, and has been developed throughout her subsequent work.

Cosmopolitics is deliberately set against "cosmopolitanism." Where cosmopolitanism seeks a universal human community -- a politics based on a shared human nature or shared human reason -- cosmopolitics insists that the cosmos itself is multiple, contested, and not pre-given. The "cosmos" of cosmopolitics is not a harmonious whole but a pluriverse -- a world made up of many different kinds of beings, many different practices, many different ways of inhabiting the world.

For Stengers, "we are not alone" -- a phrase that recurs throughout her work and encapsulates her process-ontological commitment to the agency and reality of non-human beings. The world is populated not just by human beings but by gods, spirits, ecosystems, microbes, scientific instruments, political institutions, and the myriad entities brought into being by the diverse practices that compose our world's pluriverse.

This is process philosophy taken into the political arena: if reality is processual, if the cosmos is not a pre-given container but an ongoing achievement, then politics must be understood not as the governance of an already-constituted community but as the ongoing negotiation of what kind of world will be brought into being -- which entities will count, which will be heard, which will be allowed to persist.

Stengers' cosmopolitics is profoundly influenced by Bruno Latour's actor-network theory and by the work of Eduardo Viveiros de Castro on Amerindian perspectivism. But Stengers adds a distinctly Whiteheadian dimension: the insistence that every entity -- human, non-human, natural, artificial -- has its own "power to affect and be affected," its own perspective, its own way of participating in the becoming of the world.

### 2.3 The Ecology of Practices

Stengers' concept of the "ecology of practices" is another key contribution to contemporary process philosophy. Practices -- scientific, artistic, political, religious, technical -- are not merely human activities. They are modes of world-making, of bringing certain kinds of entities into existence, of creating specific kinds of relations between the practitioner and the world.

An ecology of practices asks: What kinds of beings does this practice bring into existence? What demands does this practice place on its practitioners? What kinds of relations does it make possible and impossible? How does it relate to other practices? This is process ontology applied to the analysis of knowledge production: scientific facts, mathematical entities, and technological artifacts are not discovered but produced through specific practices, and these practices themselves become part of the ongoing process of world-making.

Stengers is particularly critical of what she calls "modern science" in its dominant form -- not because it is unreliable or wrong, but because it has become hegemonic, silencing other modes of knowing and other kinds of beings. Her work on "slow science" advocates for a more careful, more attentive, more responsive mode of scientific practice -- one that recognizes its own processual character and its participation in the becoming of the world it claims merely to describe.

### 2.4 Non-Human Agency and the Pluriverse

Stengers' insistence on non-human agency is a direct extension of Whitehead's metaphysics into contemporary debates about the nature of scientific practice, environmental politics, and technology. In *The Invention of Modern Science* (2000) and *Cosmopolitics I & II* (2003), Stengers argues that scientific objects are not passively discovered but actively constructed through practices that bring them into being and sustain them.

This is not social constructivism in the standard sense. Stengers does not deny the reality of scientific objects; she insists that their reality is constituted through the practices that produce and sustain them. The electron is as real as the chair, but its reality is bound up with the specific practices of particle physics. Reality is not a single, pre-given world but a pluriverse of realities, each sustained by its own practices, each in negotiation and conflict with the others.

### 2.5 Stengers and Contemporary Process Philosophy

Stengers' work represents what might be called the "political turn" in contemporary process philosophy. Where Whitehead's metaphysics was speculative and systematic, and where Deleuze's was focused on the liberation of desire and the critique of representation, Stengers' cosmopolitics takes process philosophy into the urgent, practical questions of environmental crisis, scientific authority, technological governance, and the future of life on Earth.

Her influence is felt across science and technology studies (STS), political ecology, anthropology, and continental philosophy. She has been a major influence on Bruno Latour, who himself has moved toward a more processual ontology in his later work, and on the "ontological turn" in contemporary anthropology associated with Arturo Escobar, Philippe Descola, and others who emphasize the plurality of worlds.

## 3. Nicholas Rescher: Analytic Process Metaphysics

Nicholas Rescher (b. 1928) is the most significant contemporary figure in the analytic tradition of process philosophy. Where Whitehead and Deleuze represent the speculative and continental strands of process thought respectively, Rescher has developed a rigorous, formalized, technically sophisticated version of process metaphysics that speaks the language of analytic philosophy.

### 3.1 Categorial Processualism

Rescher's central project in *Process Philosophy* (1996) and *Process Metaphysics* (2008) is to establish process as the fundamental ontological category. He calls this "categorial processualism" -- the position that process is not merely one category among others but the foundational category from which all others can be derived.

In contrast to substance categorialism -- the dominant tradition from Aristotle through Descartes to Quine -- which takes substance (thing, object, individual) as the primary ontological unit and explains process as a secondary phenomenon (change *of* substances), Rescher reverses the explanatory order. Substances and things are not the primary units of reality; they are patterns, regularities, or stable configurations within processes. What we call a "thing" is a process of sufficient stability and coherence to be identified as a distinct entity.

Rescher defines a process ontologically: "A process is a collection of changes that constitutes a dynamic system. It is not a thing that changes but change itself organized into a temporally unified and coherent whole." This is a crucial distinction from the common-sense notion of a process: Rescher is not talking about "the process of building a house" but about process as a fundamental ontological category -- a mode of being rather than an event that happens *to* beings.

### 3.2 Process as the Explanandum and Explanans

Rescher argues that process can serve as both the explanandum (the thing to be explained) and the explanans (the thing that does the explaining). Processes explain the world not by reducing it to static entities but by showing how the stability and identity we observe are products of dynamic processes.

He identifies several key features of processes that distinguish them analytically from substances:

1. **Temporal spread:** Processes are inherently temporal; they cannot be analyzed instantaneously.
2. **Internal differentiation:** Processes have internal structure -- phases, stages, components -- that are themselves processual.
3. **Functional characterization:** Processes are defined by what they do (their "work") rather than by what they are (their properties).
4. **Irreversibility:** Many processes are irreversibly directional -- they cannot be run backward and produce the same results.
5. **Emergent properties:** Processes can generate properties and entities at higher levels of organization that cannot be predicted from their lower-level components.

### 3.3 The Logic of Process

Rescher has made significant contributions to the formalization of process thought. In *A Theory of Process* (1967) and subsequent work, he develops a "process logic" that replaces the extensional logic of standard predicate calculus with a logic that can adequately represent the dynamic, temporal, and relational character of processes.

Among his key contributions:

- **Process mereology:** The study of the part-whole relationships among processes, which differ fundamentally from the part-whole relationships among substances because processual parts are themselves processes.
- **Process taxonomy:** A classification system for different kinds of processes (developmental, recurrent, teleological, etc.) based on their temporal structure, causal organization, and normative character.
- **Process identity:** An account of when processes are identical, continuous, or distinct that does not rely on the identity of substances.

Rescher's formal approach to process logic has been influential in the development of process ontologies for computer science, artificial intelligence, and cognitive science, where the dynamics of processes cannot be adequately captured by the static representational structures of traditional symbolic logic.

### 3.4 Rescher, Pragmatism, and Process

Rescher's process metaphysics is deeply influenced by American pragmatism, particularly the work of Charles Sanders Peirce. He argues that pragmatism and process philosophy share a common insight: that reality is not a static structure to be represented but an ongoing, dynamic process in which knowledge, action, and reality itself are co-constituted.

For Rescher, the processual character of reality explains why human knowledge is always fallible, always provisional, always in development. We do not have access to a complete and timeless representation of reality because reality itself is not a complete and timeless structure but an ongoing, never-finished process. This epistemological consequence of process metaphysics has implications not only for philosophy but for science, technology, and practical decision-making.

### 3.5 Rescher's Place in Contemporary Philosophy

Rescher's analytic approach to process philosophy serves as a crucial bridge between the continental and analytic traditions. While his work is deeply engaged with Whitehead, Whitehead's philosophy is notoriously difficult to formalize and has often been dismissed by analytic philosophers as unsystematic or obscure. Rescher demonstrates that the core insights of process philosophy can be articulated with the precision and rigor expected in analytic philosophy.

His work has been influential among analytic philosophers interested in metaphysics and the philosophy of science, among process philosophers seeking to dialogue with the analytic tradition, and among cognitive scientists and computer scientists looking for a philosophically rigorous foundation for process-based approaches to these fields.

## 4. N. Katherine Hayles: Posthuman Cognitive Assemblages and Technotexts

N. Katherine Hayles (b. 1943) is one of the most important contemporary theorists working at the intersection of process philosophy, media theory, cognitive science, and literary studies. Her early work on chaos theory and posthumanism laid the groundwork for her more recent investigations into cognitive assemblages, technoromanticism, and the processual character of computational media.

### 4.1 How We Became Posthuman: Embodied Virtuality

Hayles' most famous work, *How We Became Posthuman* (1999), traces the history of the concept of information in cybernetics, molecular biology, and literature, arguing that the "posthuman" is not a future state to be achieved but a historical development already underway. The posthuman, for Hayles, is not a disembodied, information-based entity floating in cyberspace -- this is the liberal humanist subject recast in informational terms. Rather, the posthuman acknowledges that the boundaries between human and machine, organism and environment, information and materiality are porous, fluid, and always already in process.

The key shift Hayles identifies is from a metaphysics of "presence and absence" (the binary logic of the digital) to a metaphysics of "emergence and dissipation" (the dynamic, processual logic of complex systems). This is a process-philosophical revolution at the level of the technical imaginary: the way we conceptualize information, computation, and intelligence itself shifts from static, discrete units to dynamic, emergent processes.

### 4.2 Nonconscious Cognition and Material Agency

In her later work, particularly *Unthought: The Power of the Implicit Nonconscious* (2017), Hayles develops the concept of "nonconscious cognition" -- forms of cognition that are not identical with human consciousness but are nonetheless real, processual, and agential. This includes the "cognition" of bacteria, of cells, of ecosystems, and especially of computational systems.

Drawing on the enactivist approach in cognitive science (Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson), Hayles argues that cognition is not computation in the classical sense but a dynamic, embodied process of sense-making through interaction with an environment. Computational systems participate in this ecology of cognition: they "think" not in the human sense of conscious deliberation but in the processual sense of pattern recognition, adaptive response, and structural coupling with their environments.

This is process philosophy applied to cognitive science: cognition is not a thing that happens in brains but a process of dynamic interaction between embodied agents and their environments. The computational revolution has made this processual account visible at a technological level, revealing the inadequacy of representational models of mind (the "brain as computer" metaphor) and pointing toward a more dynamic, embodied, and embedded understanding of intelligence.

### 4.3 Technotexts and Embodied Virtuality

Hayles' concept of the "technotext" -- literary works that thematize and enact the processes by which technology produces meaning -- is a process-philosophical approach to literary studies. In *Writing Machines* (2002), she argues that technotexts (such as ergodic literature, digital poetry, and multimedia installations) cannot be understood through traditional literary criticism, which treats the text as a stable object to be interpreted. Instead, they require a process-oriented approach that attends to the material, technological, and performative dimensions of meaning-making.

The technotext is not a thing but an event -- a process of meaning-production that unfolds only in the interaction between reader, text, and technology. This is a deeply processual conception of literary meaning, one that echoes Bergson's duration, Whitehead's actual occasions, and Deleuze's becoming.

### 4.4 Cognitive Assemblages

In *Unthought* and subsequent work, Hayles introduces the concept of "cognitive assemblages" -- technical ensembles involving humans, nonhumans, and computational systems that together constitute complex cognitive dynamics. A cognitive assemblage might be a financial trading network, a military drone system, a social media platform, or a scientific research community.

The key insight is that cognition is distributed across these assemblages -- it is not located in any individual brain or processor but emerges from the processual interactions of all the components. This is a process-philosophical understanding of cognition: it is an emergent, dynamic, relational phenomenon that cannot be reduced to any of its component parts. The cognitive assemblage is a process, not a thing.

### 4.5 Hayles and Contemporary Process Philosophy

Hayles' work represents a distinctive contemporary development of process philosophy: its application to the analysis of computational media, cognitive science, and literary culture. She does not frame herself as a "process philosopher" in the traditional sense, but her work is deeply informed by process thought -- particularly the Bergsonian and Whiteheadian traditions -- and she explicitly engages with process philosophy in her discussions of emergence, dynamic systems, and the critique of representational thinking.

Her work has been influential in digital humanities, media studies, cognitive literary studies, and the emerging field of posthumanist studies.

## 5. Process and Science: Prigogine, Complexity, and Quantum Processuality

Process philosophy has always claimed that its metaphysical claims are supported by, and in turn illuminate, the findings of the natural sciences. This section examines two key domains where the science-process philosophy connection is most evident: thermodynamics and the science of complex systems (associated with the work of Ilya Prigogine), and quantum field theory.

### 5.1 Ilya Prigogine: Dissipative Structures and the End of Certainty

Ilya Prigogine (1917-2003), Nobel laureate in chemistry (1977), developed the theory of dissipative structures -- open systems that maintain their organization far from thermodynamic equilibrium by continuously exchanging energy and matter with their environment. This theory has profound implications for process philosophy.

**The End of Certainty:** Prigogine's book *The End of Certainty* (1997, with Isabelle Stengers, who was his student) is one of the most significant interdisciplinary syntheses of process philosophy and science. The book argues that classical physics -- from Newton through Einstein -- presupposed a reversible, deterministic universe where time is a parameter rather than a genuine feature of reality. Prigogine's work on non-equilibrium thermodynamics and chaos theory shows that irreversibility is not a secondary effect or a consequence of our ignorance but a fundamental feature of reality.

**Dissipative Structures as Process Ontology Made Empirical:** A dissipative structure (such as a hurricane, a living cell, or a chemical reaction oscillating far from equilibrium) is a process that maintains itself through continuous exchange with its environment. It is not a substance or an object but a self-organizing pattern -- a process that persists not despite its instability but because of it. This is process philosophy in its most literal, empirical form: the stable entities of everyday experience (organisms, ecosystems, even stars) are dissipative structures, sustained by ongoing processes of energy flow and material cycling.

**The Constructive Role of Instability:** Prigogine's most radical insight is that instability and irreversibility are not merely destructive but constructive. Far from equilibrium, small fluctuations can be amplified into new patterns and structures -- a phenomenon called "bifurcation." This means that novelty, creativity, and the emergence of genuinely new forms are not anomalies but expected features of complex, far-from-equilibrium systems. This is process philosophy made scientific: the world is not moving toward a predetermined endpoint but is genuinely creative, producing novel forms and patterns that were not prefigured in the prior state of the system.

### 5.2 Complex Systems Theory as Process Metaphysics

The science of complexity -- encompassing chaos theory, self-organization, adaptive systems, and network science -- provides a scientific framework that is deeply congenial to process philosophy. Complex systems are characterized by:

- **Non-linearity:** Effects are disproportionate to causes; small changes can produce massive consequences (the "butterfly effect").
- **Emergence:** Properties and behaviors arise at the system level that cannot be predicted from or reduced to the properties and behaviors of individual components.
- **Self-organization:** Order arises spontaneously from the interactions of components without a central organizer.
- **Adaptation:** Complex systems learn, evolve, and respond to their environments.

Each of these characteristics is a direct expression of process ontology: the world is not an assembly of discrete objects but a nested hierarchy of processes, each of which exhibits emergent properties, self-organization, and adaptive capacity.

### 5.3 Quantum Field Theory and Process Ontology

Quantum field theory (QFT) is arguably the most successful physical theory ever developed, and it is fundamentally processual. In QFT, the basic entities are not particles but fields -- continuous, dynamic entities that pervade all of spacetime. What we call "particles" are excitations, disturbances, or quanta of these fields.

Ontologically, this means:

- **Particles are not substances:** They are transient excitations of underlying fields.
- **Fields are not static:** They are dynamic entities governed by equations that describe their evolution, interaction, and fluctuation.
- **Vacuum is not empty:** The quantum vacuum is a seething cauldron of virtual particle-antiparticle pairs, constantly fluctuating and interacting.

The process-philosophical implications are profound. The ontology of quantum field theory is closer to Heraclitus' "ever-living fire" or Whitehead's "actual occasions" than it is to the billiard-ball atoms of classical physics. Reality at its most fundamental level is not a collection of static entities but a dynamic field of ongoing processes, interactions, and fluctuations.

The vacuum fluctuations of QFT are particularly striking from a process-philosophical perspective: even in the absence of "real" particles, the field is active, productive, and dynamic. The void is not emptiness but the plenitude of processual potentiality -- not unlike Deleuze's virtual, which is a real, full, differential field that produces actual entities through a creative, non-deterministic process.

### 5.4 Whitehead and Quantum Mechanics

The relationship between Whitehead's process philosophy and quantum mechanics has been extensively explored by process philosophers and physicists alike. Whitehead developed his metaphysics in the 1920s, contemporaneously with the development of quantum theory, and many of his concepts -- quantum jumps as "actual occasions," the non-locality of "prehensions," the processual nature of "extensive continuum" -- have been argued to anticipate or parallel developments in quantum physics.

David Bohm's implicate/explicate order, Werner Heisenberg's "potentia," and Carlo Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics all share a process-philosophical sensibility. Reality, in these accounts, is fundamentally relational, processual, and contextual -- not a collection of substances with determinate properties but a network of relations, processes, and events.

## 6. Process, AI, and Computing: Neural Networks as Processual Ontologies

The relationship between process philosophy and computing/AI is one of the most active and productive areas of contemporary interdisciplinary research. This section examines the processual character of computational systems and their implications for both philosophy and technology.

### 6.1 Connectionism and Neural Networks as Process

Connectionist models of computation -- especially artificial neural networks -- represent a shift from the classical symbolic (GOFAI -- "Good Old-Fashioned AI") model of computation to a process-oriented model. Classical AI treats cognition as the manipulation of discrete symbols according to formal rules, a model that closely mirrors substance ontology: symbols are the "things" that are manipulated, rules are the "laws" that govern their behavior.

Neural networks, by contrast, are fundamentally processual. They do not manipulate discrete symbols but consist of distributed, dynamic patterns of activation across interconnected nodes. Knowledge in a neural network is not stored in any particular node or synapse but is distributed across the entire network as a pattern of weights and connections. Cognition in a neural network is not a sequence of discrete computational steps but a continuous, dynamic flow of activation.

This processual character is particularly evident in modern deep learning systems, where the "knowledge" acquired during training is distributed across billions of parameters, none of which can be individually identified as representing any particular concept. The system is a process -- a self-organizing, adaptive, dynamic system -- rather than a repository of representations.

### 6.2 Dynamic Systems Theory in Cognitive Science

Dynamic systems theory (DST) in cognitive science, associated with the work of Esther Thelen, Tim van Gelder, and Randall Beer, provides a process-philosophical framework for understanding cognition that is explicitly anti-representational and anti-computational (in the classical sense). DST models cognition as a complex, non-linear dynamical system that undergoes continuous, time-evolving changes in its state space.

The key insight of DST is that cognitive behavior can be modeled as trajectories through a state space -- not as the execution of programs or the manipulation of symbols, but as the evolution of a dynamic system through a landscape of attractors, repellers, and bifurcations. This is process philosophy applied to the most intimate of human phenomena: thinking, acting, and perceiving are all dynamic processes, not the manipulation of static representations.

### 6.3 Machine Learning as Process: Training, Inference, and Emergence

The processual character of machine learning systems extends beyond their architecture to their entire lifecycle:

- **Training:** The process of gradient descent through a loss landscape is itself a dynamic, iterative, non-linear process. The system is not learning "facts" or "rules" but adjusting its internal parameters through a continuous process of error minimization. The training process is path-dependent: identical architectures initialized with different random seeds can develop qualitatively different internal representations.
- **Inference:** Even "at rest," a neural network is not a static repository. Each inference is a dynamic process of activation flowing through layers, producing outputs that are sensitive to the precise state of every parameter.
- **Emergence:** Scaling laws in large language models and other AI systems demonstrate that qualitative capabilities emerge at certain scales of parameter count and training data -- an emergent property that is quintessentially process-philosophical. The capabilities of a 70B-parameter model are not merely "more" of the capabilities of a 7B-parameter model; they are qualitatively different, exhibiting emergent properties (e.g., in-context learning, chain-of-thought reasoning) that were not present at smaller scales.

### 6.4 Process Philosophy of AI

Several scholars have begun to develop explicitly process-philosophical approaches to AI:

- **Cognitive assemblages (Hayles):** AI systems are not autonomous agents but cognitive assemblages -- processual ensembles that include humans, data, algorithms, hardware, institutional structures, and socio-technical environments. Cognition emerges from the interactions of all these components.
- **Process ontology of computation:** Scholars like Johanna Seibt have argued for a "process ontology of computation" that understands computation not as symbol manipulation but as a dynamic, ongoing process of information transformation -- closer to Whitehead's actual occasions than to Turing's discrete tape symbols.
- **AI and embodiment:** The process-philosophical critique of disembodied AI (the "brain in a vat" model of intelligence) has been vindicated by the increasing recognition in AI research of the importance of embodied cognition, situated learning, and sensorimotor coupling.

### 6.5 AI and the Future of Process Philosophy

The development of artificial intelligence presents both challenges and opportunities for process philosophy:

- **Challenge:** If AI systems can perform tasks traditionally associated with human cognition (reasoning, pattern recognition, language generation), what does this mean for the process-philosophical claim that cognition is embodied, situated, and emergent? Current large language models, for example, exhibit impressive linguistic capabilities despite lacking bodies and situated environments.
- **Opportunity:** The processual character of AI systems -- particularly large-scale, distributed, adaptive systems -- provides empirical evidence for process ontology. These systems are fundamentally processual, fundamentally dynamic, and fundamentally non-substantial. They challenge the representational paradigm of cognitive science and point toward a more process-oriented understanding of intelligence.

## 7. Contemporary Debates: OOO, Speculative Realism, New Materialism

The contemporary landscape of continental metaphysics is marked by intense debate among various movements, each of which has a complex relationship to process philosophy.

### 7.1 Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO) and the Critique of Process

Object-Oriented Ontology, founded by Graham Harman and developed by thinkers including Quentin Meillassoux, Levi Bryant, Timothy Morton, and Ian Bogost, represents the most sustained and systematic critique of process philosophy in contemporary metaphysics.

**Flat Ontology vs. Process Ontology:** Harman's central thesis is that ontology should be "flat" -- all entities (whether quarks, chairs, nations, or fictional characters) exist equally and cannot be reduced to one another. This flat ontology is set against what Harman calls "overmining" (reducing entities to their relations, effects, or processes) and "undermining" (reducing entities to their components or underlying structure).

From Harman's perspective, process philosophy is guilty of overmining: it reduces entities to their relations, processes, and interactions. A chair is not a process of becoming; it is a chair, a real object with its own integrity and withdrawn reality -- the reality that cannot be exhausted by any of its relations or processes.

Harman writes: "Objects are never exhausted by their relations or their components. A real object always contains surplus, or a hidden depth that remains withdrawn from all access." This withdrawn reality -- the object's "reality in itself" -- is what process philosophy, with its emphasis on relations, processes, and becoming, fails to account for.

**The Process Response:** Process philosophers have responded to the OOO critique in several ways:

1. **Redefining process:** Process philosophers argue that OOO mischaracterizes process ontology by equating it with "undermining" or "overmining." A sophisticated process ontology does not reduce entities to processes; it recognizes that entities themselves *are* processes -- complex, multi-scale, temporally extended patterns of activity that have genuine reality and integrity.
2. **The reality of relations:** Process philosophers argue that relations are not secondary to entities but constitutive of them. The relational constitution of entities does not diminish their reality; it is the very condition of their reality.
3. **Temporal depth:** Some process philosophers argue that OOO's withdrawal thesis can be reinterpreted processually: what Harman calls the "withdrawn" reality of the object is simply its irreducible temporal depth -- its history, its virtuality, its processual becoming. The object is withdrawn not because it transcends all relations but because it is an ongoing process that can never be fully captured at any single moment.

**Key Debate: Harman vs. Process:** The debate between Harman and process philosophers is the most active in contemporary speculative realism. Harman has published direct critiques of process philosophy, particularly of Whitehead, arguing that Whitehead's "actual occasions" are too processual and relational to account for the withdrawn, autonomous reality of objects. Process philosophers, in turn, argue that Harman's objects are too static and isolated, lacking the dynamic, relational, and creative dimensions that process philosophy brings to the fore.

### 7.2 Speculative Realism and Process Philosophy

Speculative realism -- the broader movement that includes OOO, Ray Brassier's eliminative materialism, Iain Hamilton Grant's Schellingianism, and Quentin Meillassoux's "speculative materialism" -- emerged in the late 2000s as a reaction against the Kantian "correlationist" turn in continental philosophy. While speculative realism is not uniformly pro- or anti-process, several of its key figures engage deeply with process philosophy:

- **Iain Hamilton Grant:** Grant's *Philosophies of Nature after Schelling* (2006) is a sustained engagement with process philosophy via Schelling's Naturphilosophie. Grant argues that Schelling's philosophy of nature -- which treats nature as a productive, dynamic, self-organizing process (natura naturans) rather than a collection of products (natura naturata) -- provides the resources for a contemporary materialism that is both speculative and processual.
- **Reza Negarestani:** In *Intelligence and Spirit* (2018), Negarestani develops a rationalist account of intelligence as a process -- not a substance or a static property -- of self-correction, self-determination, and self-actualization. This is a process account of cognition that stands in creative tension with both OOO and the enactivist approach.
- **Quentin Meillassoux:** While Meillassoux's *After Finitude* (2006) is not explicitly process-philosophical, his concept of "hyperchaos" -- an absolute, lawless contingency that undermines any necessary order -- resonates with process philosophy's emphasis on the creative, unprefigured character of becoming.

### 7.3 New Materialism: Process-Adjacent Thought

New materialism -- a diverse movement encompassing the work of Jane Bennett, Karen Barad, Manuel DeLanda, Rosi Braidotti, and Stacy Alaimo -- shares significant affinities with process philosophy while maintaining its own distinctive commitments.

**Jane Bennett: Vibrant Matter:** In *Vibrant Matter: A Political Ecology of Things* (2010), Bennett develops a "vital materialism" that attributes agency, vitality, and creativity to non-human matter. This is deeply process-philosophical: Bennett's matter is not dead, passive stuff but active, self-organizing, creative material that participates in the ongoing production of the world. Her key concept of "thing-power" -- the capacity of things to act as quasi-agents with their own trajectories, propensities, and tendencies -- is a direct extension of process ontology into political philosophy and ecological thought.

Bennett draws explicitly on Whitehead, Spinoza, Deleuze, and Henri Bergson in developing her vital materialism. She acknowledges that her work owes a debt to process philosophy while seeking to ground it in a more materialist, less spiritual, ontology.

**Karen Barad: Agential Realism:** Barad's *Meeting the Universe Halfway* (2007) develops "agential realism" -- an ontology that combines quantum physics, feminist theory, and a process-relational metaphysics inspired by Niels Bohr and Donna Haraway. Barad's key concept is "intra-action": entities do not pre-exist their relations; they emerge dynamically through intra-actions (a term she coins to distinguish her position from the relational ontology of "interaction").

Barad's agential realism is perhaps the most sophisticated contemporary development of process philosophy in feminist and STS contexts. Her notion of the "apparatus" -- the material-discursive arrangement through which entities are brought into being -- is a process-ontological concept: the apparatus is not a thing but a dynamic, material, performative arrangement that produces entities through its ongoing activity.

**Manuel DeLanda: Assemblage Theory:** DeLanda's *A New Philosophy of Society* (2006) and *Assemblage Theory and Its Social Implications* (2016) develop a process-oriented social ontology based on Deleuze and Guattari's concept of the assemblage. For DeLanda, social entities (cities, nations, organizations, markets) are assemblages -- contingent gatherings of heterogeneous elements held together by specific relations and processes. Assemblages are inherently processual: they have histories, they change, they can be decomposed and recomposed. They are not substances or structures but dynamic, ongoing processes.

DeLanda's contribution is to ground process ontology in a framework that is accessible to social scientists and philosophers of social science, demonstrating the applicability of process philosophy beyond the traditional domains of metaphysics and natural philosophy.

### 7.4 The Process vs. Object Debate as the Central Contemporary Metaphysical Question

The contemporary metaphysical landscape is defined by the debate between process and object-oriented approaches. While these two movements share a commitment to moving beyond the limitations of post-Kantian correlationism and a desire to take seriously the reality of non-human entities and processes, they diverge sharply on the fundamental question of ontology:

- **Process philosophers** argue that the fundamental units of reality are processes, events, and becomings. Entities are not primary; they are patterns and organizations within processes.
- **OOO philosophers** argue that objects are fundamental and that their reality is not exhausted by their relations, processes, or qualities.

This debate is not merely academic. It has profound implications for how we understand the natural world, technology, politics, ethics, and the future of human civilization. An ontology of processes leads to a politics of relations, an ethics of care, and an understanding of technology as participatory world-making. An ontology of objects leads to a politics of coexistence, an ethics of withdrawal and respect, and an understanding of technology as a domain of autonomous entities.

The ongoing dialogue and tension between these approaches -- and between process philosophy itself and the adjacent movements of new materialism and speculative realism -- is one of the most productive and exciting areas of contemporary metaphysical inquiry.

---

## Synthesis and Conclusion: The Process Philosophy of the Twenty-First Century

Contemporary process philosophy is a vibrant, pluralistic, and interdisciplinary tradition that extends well beyond the boundaries of academic metaphysics. It encompasses:

- **Continental process philosophy** (Deleuze, Stengers) that extends process thought into political, ecological, and cultural domains, emphasizing the creative, non-identitarian, and pluriversal character of being.
- **Analytic process philosophy** (Rescher) that provides rigorous, formalized accounts of process ontology and process logic, bridging the gap between analytic and continental traditions.
- **Science-informed process philosophy** (Hayles, Prigogine) that grounds process metaphysics in the findings of thermodynamics, complexity theory, quantum physics, cognitive science, and computational theory.
- **Process-adjacent movements** (new materialism, agential realism, assemblage theory) that draw on process philosophy for their own distinctive contributions to contemporary thought.

The common thread uniting these diverse approaches is the conviction that reality is fundamentally processual -- that becoming is primary, that relations are constitutive, that novelty is genuine, and that the world is an ongoing, unfinished process of creation and differentiation.

As the challenges of the twenty-first century -- from artificial intelligence to ecological crisis, from technological governance to the rethinking of human agency -- demand new philosophical frameworks, process philosophy offers a robust, empirically grounded, and ethically engaged metaphysical alternative to the substance-oriented thinking that has dominated Western philosophy for millennia. It is not merely a metaphysical doctrine but a way of inhabiting, engaging with, and participating in the ongoing becoming of the world.
