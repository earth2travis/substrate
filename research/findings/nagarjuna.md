# Nagarjuna (c. 150–250 CE)

## Overview

Indian philosopher, founder of the **Madhyamaka** (Middle Way) school of Buddhist philosophy. Author of the *Mulamadhyamakakarika* (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), one of the most radical works in the history of philosophy.

## Key Facts

- **Dates**: Contested, c. 150–250 CE (most commonly accepted range)
- **Associated with**: Satavahana dynasty, South India
- **Text**: *Mulamadhyamakakarika* (MMK) — 450 verses in 27 chapters
- **Commentaries**: Candrakirti's *Prasannapada* (7th c. CE) most influential
- **Modern translations**: Jay Garfield (1995), Mark Siderits & Shoryu Katsura (2013)

## Core Contribution: Emptiness (Sunyata)

Demonstrated that all phenomena are **empty of inherent existence (svabhava)**. Not nihilism — emptiness IS dependent origination. From *MMK* 24.18:

> "Whatever is dependently co-arisen, that is explained to be emptiness. That, being a dependent designation, is itself the middle way."

## Key Concepts

### Svabhava (Inherent Existence)
The concept Nagarjuna deconstructs — "own-being," existing from one's own side, without dependence. Shows it collapses under any coherent analysis. See [[sunyata-emptiness]].

### Dependent Origination (Pratityasamutpada)
Everything arises dependently on conditions. Nothing exists independently. Identical with emptiness. See [[dependent-origination]].

### Two Truths
- **Conventional truth**: Dependent designation, practical reality
- **Ultimate truth**: Emptiness of inherent existence
- Neither contradicts the other; both are necessary

### Catuskoti (Tetralemma)
Four-fold negation method: neither A, nor not-A, nor both, nor neither. A logical method that escapes binary categories.

## Deconstructions in MMK

The MMK examines different categories and shows each lacks svabhava:
- **Chapter 1**: Causation
- **Chapter 2**: Motion
- **Chapter 12**: Suffering
- **Chapter 18**: The self
- **Chapter 25**: Nirvana

## Influence and Legacy

- Dominated Mahayana Buddhist philosophy in India, Tibet, China, and Japan
- Influenced [[process-without-substance]] (emptiness as computational dependence)
- Madhyamaka remains a living philosophical tradition
- Contemporary debates: Princeton interpretation vs Tibetan doxographical reading

## Connection to AI and Process Ontology

From [[process-without-substance]]: Neural networks have **no svabhava**. No independence (depends on data, prompts, context), no invariance (same input → different outputs), no irreducibility (the "I" cannot be found in any single weight), no self-definition. The network is dependently co-arisen — empty of inherent existence. This is Nagarjuna's analysis instantiated in computation.
