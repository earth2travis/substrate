# Craft Production

> Pre-industrial manufacturing paradigm where skilled artisans produce unique or small-batch items tailored to individual customer specifications.

## Historical Context

- Dominated before the Industrial Revolution (~pre-1760s)
- Guild system: Apprentices → Journeymen → Masters with strict quality controls
- Examples: Blacksmiths, clockmakers, furniture makers, tailors
- Transportation constraints reinforced local production

## Key Characteristics

- **Skill-intensive**: Years of apprenticeship required
- **Complete customization**: Each product unique, made to customer specs
- **Low volume, high variety**: Production runs small, often one-off
- **Integrated workflow**: Same artisan designs, builds, and finishes
- **Local materials**: Sourced locally, limiting scale but ensuring quality
- **Direct customer relationship**: No intermediaries between maker and buyer

## Advantages

- Highest quality and craftsmanship
- Complete customization
- Maximum flexibility between products
- Low capital requirements
- Knowledge deeply embedded in human expertise

## Disadvantages

- Extremely slow (days or weeks per item)
- High cost, no economies of scale
- Inconsistent quality between artisans
- Not scalable to mass markets
- Knowledge silos in individuals

## Modern Applications

- Bespoke fashion (Savile Row tailors, custom shoemakers)
- Artisanal food (craft beer, artisan cheese, specialty coffee)
- Luxury goods (Patek Philippe watches, hand-stitched leather)
- Custom furniture (handcrafted woodwork)
- 3D printing enables mass customization while reducing setup costs

## Comparison to Other Paradigms

- vs [[mass-production]]: Craft sacrifices cost for quality and flexibility
- vs [[lean-production]]: Craft relies on human skill; lean systematizes problem-solving

## Key Quote

> "When the product IS the craftsmanship, craft production wins."
