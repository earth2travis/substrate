# Mass Production

> Early 20th century manufacturing paradigm relying on standardized parts, assembly lines, and specialized labor to produce large volumes of identical goods at low cost.

## Historical Context

- **Ford Motor Company (1913)**: First moving assembly line for Model T
- **Scientific Management (Taylorism)**: Frederick Taylor's time-motion studies
- **Interchangeable parts**: Eli Whitney's firearms manufacturing
- **Peak era**: 1920s-1970s, dominated Western industrial economies

## Key Characteristics

- **Standardization**: Identical parts enable interchangeable components
- **Assembly line**: Sequential workstations with single-task workers
- **Division of labor**: Narrow, repetitive tasks per worker
- **High volume, low variety**: Few variants, maximum output
- **Dedicated machinery**: Specialized equipment for specific operations
- **Economies of scale**: Unit cost decreases dramatically with volume

## The Ford Model T Example

- **Production time**: Dropped from 12.5 hours to 93 minutes per car by 1914
- **Price reduction**: From $850 (1908) to $260 (1925)
- **Volume**: 15 million Model T's produced by 1927
- **Quote**: "Any customer can have a car painted any color he wants so long as it is black"

## Advantages

- Dramatically lower costs at scale
- High production speed (thousands per day)
- Consistent, standardized quality
- Makes goods affordable to masses
- Large-scale employment opportunity

## Disadvantages

- Inflexible - changing products requires expensive retooling
- High inventory costs (large batches require storage)
- Worker alienation from repetitive tasks
- Quality issues propagate before detection
- Overproduction and excess inventory waste
- Capital intensive (massive machinery investment needed)
- Push production creates goods customers may not want

## Comparison to Other Paradigms

- vs [[craft-production]]: Mass sacrifices flexibility and customization for cost and scale
- vs [[lean-production]]: Mass pushes inventory; lean pulls based on demand
