# Lean Production

> Post-WWII manufacturing paradigm focused on eliminating waste, maximizing customer value, and continuously improving processes. Originated at Toyota as the Toyota Production System (TPS).

## Historical Context

- **Toyota Production System (TPS)**: Developed by Taiichi Ohno at Toyota after WWII
- **Resource scarcity**: Post-war Japan had limited capital and materials
- **MIT IMVP Study**: 5-year, $5M research on global auto manufacturers
- **The Machine That Changed the World (1990)**: Womack, Jones, Roos coined "Lean Production"
- **Spread to West**: Adopted globally in 1990s and 2000s

## Key Characteristics

- **Waste elimination**: Seven wastes (muda) systematically removed
- **Just-in-Time (JIT)**: Production/delivery only when needed
- **Continuous flow**: Uninterrupted work movement through process
- **Pull system**: Production triggered by customer demand, not forecasts
- **Quality at source**: Problems caught and fixed immediately
- **Continuous improvement (Kaizen)**: Incremental improvements by all employees
- **Respect for people**: Workers are problem-solvers, not just task performers

## Two TPS Pillars

### Just-in-Time (JIT)
- Make only what is needed, when needed, in the amount needed
- Pull production based on customer orders
- Kanban cards signal when to produce/reorder
- Takt time sets production rhythm to customer demand

### Jidoka (Autonomation)
- Build quality into the process
- Machines stop automatically when problems occur
- Workers can stop the line (Andon cord) to fix issues
- Separate human work from machine work

## The Seven Wastes (Muda)

1. **Transportation**: Unnecessary movement of materials
2. **Inventory**: Excess raw materials, WIP, or finished goods
3. **Motion**: Unnecessary movement of people or equipment
4. **Waiting**: Idle time between process steps
5. **Overproduction**: Making more than needed or earlier than needed
6. **Overprocessing**: Doing more work than the customer requires
7. **Defects**: Rework, scrap, and inspection costs

## Key Tools and Concepts

### Chief Engineer System (Shusa)
The **Shusa** (Chief Engineer) is a single individual with end-to-end responsibility for a vehicle program — the "generalist among specialists." Not a traditional manager but a product visionary who integrates across functional boundaries. See [[chief-engineer-system]] for full details.

- **Kanban**: Visual work and inventory management
- **5S**: Sort, Straighten, Sweep, Standardize, Sustain
- **Value Stream Mapping**: Visual analysis of material and information flow
- **Poka-Yoke**: Mistake-proofing mechanisms
- **Heijunka**: Production leveling to smooth demand variation
- **Andon**: Visual signals for real-time problem identification
- **Gemba**: "Go to the actual place" -- observe work where it happens

## Advantages

- Lower inventory costs (JIT reduces capital tied up in stock)
- Higher quality (problems caught and fixed immediately)
- Greater flexibility (smaller batches enable faster changes)
- Employee empowerment (workers solve problems)
- Reduced lead times (flow-oriented processes move faster)
- Lower costs over time (continuous improvement)
- Better customer alignment (pull system)

## Disadvantages

- **Supply chain vulnerability**: JIT relies on reliable suppliers
- **Implementation difficulty**: Requires cultural change and long-term commitment
- **Initial costs**: Training, system changes, cultural transformation are expensive
- **Fragile to external shocks**: Natural disasters, pandemics disrupt JIT
- **Measurement challenges**: Cultural aspects like Kaizen are hard to quantify

## Lean and Modern Applications

- **Automotive**: Toyota remains the archetype, adopted globally
- **Consumer electronics**: Apple's supply chain
- **Healthcare**: Lean hospitals, process improvement
- **Services**: Lean banking, lean software development
- **Sustainability**: Lean's waste reduction aligns with environmental goals
