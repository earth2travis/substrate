## 1. Introduction: The Bergsonian Revolution

Henri Bergson (1859–1941) was one of the most famous and influential philosophers of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Though his international fame reached cult-like heights during his lifetime — Bertrand Russell wrote in 1912 that "every educated person is expected to be acquainted with Bergson's philosophy" — his influence waned considerably after the Second World War. It was Gilles Deleuze's 1966 book *Bergsonism* that marked the reawakening of Bergsonian scholarship, launching what has become a sustained revitalization of Bergson's thought across contemporary continental philosophy.

Bergson's philosophical project can be characterized as a sustained attempt to think **reality as process** rather than as substance. Against the dominant intellectual traditions of his day — including neo-Kantianism, positivism, mechanistic materialism, and deterministic psychology — Bergson developed an ontology of becoming in which **time (*durée*)**, not space, is the fundamental dimension of reality. His philosophy is often classified as a form of **process philosophy**, placing him in the company of Whitehead, James, and later Deleuze.

### The Core Bergsonian Movement

At the heart of Bergson's entire corpus lies a single, persistent gesture: the **refusal to reduce time to space**, the refusal to replace the continuous with the discontinuous, the refusal to substitute the living with the inert. This refusal animates every major work:

- **Time and Free Will (1889)**: The distinction between quantitative multiplicity (spatial, homogeneous) and qualitative multiplicity (temporal, heterogeneous); the defense of free will against determinism.
- **Matter and Memory (1896)**: The relationship between body and memory; the critique of psychophysical parallelism; the theory of pure memory.
- **Laughter (1900)**: The comic as "something mechanical encrusted on the living."
- **Creative Evolution (1907)**: The *élan vital*, the critique of mechanism and finalism, evolution as genuine creation.
- **The Creative Mind (1934)**: The method of intuition clarified; philosophy as the reversal of habitual thinking.
- **The Two Sources of Morality and Religion (1932)**: Closed vs. open morality; static vs. dynamic religion; the mystical experience as the culmination of evolution.

Bergson was awarded the **Nobel Prize in Literature in 1927** "in recognition of his rich and vitalizing ideas and the brilliant skill with which they have been presented." He was elected to the **Académie Française in 1914**, the first Jewish member in its history. His lectures at the Collège de France attracted enormous audiences, including T.S. Eliot, Étienne Gilson, and Jean Wahl — a testament to the extraordinary cultural reach of his thought.

## 2. Duration (Durée): The Central Concept

### 2.1 The Discovery of Duration

The concept of *durée* (duration) is the cornerstone of Bergson's philosophy. It first emerges in his 1889 doctoral thesis, *Time and Free Will: An Essay on the Immediate Data of Consciousness* (*Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience*), and it is progressively deepened and radicalized throughout his entire career.

Bergson's fundamental insight is that **time as actually lived is fundamentally different from time as measured by clocks**. Clock time — what Bergson calls "spatialized time" — treats time as a homogeneous medium in which moments succeed one another like points on a line. This conception, according to Bergson, is not really time at all; it is **space masquerading as time**.

> "We do not think real time. But we live it, because life is something other than intellect."
> — Bergson, *Creative Mind*

### 2.2 Quantitative vs. Qualitative Multiplicity

Bergson's analysis in *Time and Free Will* begins by distinguishing two fundamentally different kinds of multiplicity:

**Quantitative multiplicity** is the multiplicity of objects in space. When I count sheep, each sheep is discrete, external to the others, and interchangeable with any other. The units are homogeneous, separable, and measurable. This is the multiplicity proper to **extensive magnitudes** — the domain of spatial intuition.

**Qualitative multiplicity** is the multiplicity of states of consciousness in duration. When I listen to a melody, each note interpenetrates the others; they are not external to one another but form a continuous, heterogeneous flow. The melody is not the sum of its parts; it is an irreducible whole in which past notes linger in the present, coloring and transforming it. This is the multiplicity proper to **intensive magnitudes** — the domain of temporal experience.

> "Pure duration is the form which the succession of our conscious states assumes when our ego lets itself live, when it refrains from separating its present state from its former states."
> — Bergson, *Time and Free Will*, Chapter 2

This distinction is not merely psychological; it is **ontological**. Duration is not just how we experience time — it is what time actually *is*. The spatialization of time is an intellectual distortion, a necessary but ultimately inadequate representation that the intellect imposes on the flux of reality.

### 2.3 Duration as Continuous Flow and Qualitative Heterogeneity

Bergson describes duration with remarkable precision:

> "Duration is the continuous progress of the past which gnaws into the future and which swells as it advances."
> — Bergson, *Creative Evolution*, Chapter 1

Several features of this definition deserve careful attention:

1. **Continuity**: Duration is not composed of discrete instants. There are no "atoms" of time. The flow of duration is unbroken and indivisible. Any attempt to divide duration into instants is a retrospective intellectual act that projects spatial categories onto temporal reality.

2. **Heterogeneity**: Each moment of duration is qualitatively unique. Because the past is preserved and carried forward into the present, no two moments are ever identical. The present moment is always richer than any preceding moment because it contains all that has come before.

3. **Irreversibility**: Duration flows in one direction. The past cannot be undone; it is conserved and accumulated. This is why Bergson insists that duration is **creation** — genuinely novel and unpredictable — rather than the rearrangement of pre-existing elements.

4. **Interpenetration**: States of consciousness in duration are not external to one another (like objects in space); they interpenetrate, overlap, and transform one another. A feeling of joy is not the same joy at different moments; it evolves, deepens, or dissipates.

### 2.4 Duration and Freedom

In *Time and Free Will*, Bergson argues that the problem of free will arises only when we spatialize time. If we conceive of time as a line with past, present, and future as pre-existing points, then determinism seems inevitable: the future is already "there," and our actions are predetermined by prior causes.

But if duration is genuinely creative — if the future does not yet exist and is being continually created — then **freedom is not a property of decisions but a property of the entire personality in action**. The free act is one that springs from the whole personality, from the deep self (*le moi profond*) that exists in duration, rather than from the superficial self (*le moi superficiel*) that has been fragmented and spatialized by social conventions and intellectual habits.

> "We are free when our acts spring from our whole personality, when they express it, when they have that indefinable resemblance to it which one sometimes finds between the artist and his work."
> — Bergson, *Time and Free Will*, Chapter 4

This is not libertarian free will in the sense of indeterminism (random choice between alternatives). It is a deeper freedom — the freedom of the self to be itself, to act in accordance with the entirety of its accumulatedpast, which is always unique and always unfolding.

### 2.5 Deleuze's Reading: Duration as Multiplicity

Gilles Deleuze, in *Bergsonism* (1966), identifies **multiplicity** as Bergson's most revolutionary contribution. For Deleuze, Bergson's concept of multiplicity does "something no other philosopher had done": it unifies heterogeneity and continuity in a single concept that escapes both the One and the Many.

Deleuze identifies several key theses about duration-as-multiplicity:

1. **Duration has variations of rhythm**: Different multiplicities have different rhythms of contraction and dilation. At one extreme, a material object's "duration" is an infinite slowness of repetition — nearly instantaneous. At the other extreme, pure memory is a state of infinite dilation where the entire contracted past is present as a virtual whole. Human consciousness occupies a middle ground.

2. **Duration is not psychological**: Although first discovered in consciousness, duration is ontological. Bergson progressively extends the concept from human consciousness to all of life (*élan vital*), then to matter itself, and finally to the universe as a whole. Duration is not merely how we experience reality; it is how reality is structured.

3. **The virtual and the actual**: Deleuze emphasizes that Bergson's ontology requires the distinction between the virtual and the actual. The virtual is not the possible — the possible is a retroactive projection of the actual, a "phantom" of what could have been. The virtual, by contrast, is **real without being actual** — it is the reservoir of tendencies, potentials, and creative forces that are continually actualizing themselves in novel ways.

## 3. The Critique of Spatialized Time

### 3.1 The Cinematographic Illusion

One of Bergson's most powerful critiques of the philosophical tradition is his analysis of the **cinematographic illusion of thought**. This critique appears most prominently in *Creative Evolution* (Chapter 4) but is prepared for throughout his corpus.

Bergson compares the way the intellect approaches reality to the way a motion picture cinema produces the illusion of movement:

> "The mechanism of our ordinary knowledge is of a cinematographical kind."
> — Bergson, *Creative Evolution*, Chapter 4

In a cinema, movement is created by projecting a series of static frames in rapid succession. Each frame is an immobile snapshot; the illusion of motion arises only from their rapid juxtaposition. Similarly, the intellect approaches reality by taking "snapshots" — immobile views of a mobile reality — and then trying to reconstruct movement by stringing these views together.

This is why, Bergson argues, **Zeno's paradoxes of motion** arise. Zeno's paradox of Achilles and the tortoise assumes that to traverse a distance, one must first traverse half of it, then half of what remains, and so on infinitely. The paradox dissolves when we recognize that **movement is not composed of positions** — positions are retrospective extractions from movement, not its constituent parts.

> "The mistake of Zeno's paradoxes is that they reconstruct movement out of immobilities. They assume that movement is made up of positions, whereas positions are abstractions from movement."
> — Bergson, *Creative Evolution*

### 3.2 Analysis vs. Intuition

This cinematographic mechanism explains why **analysis can never reach the thing itself**. To analyze something is to take perspectives on it from the outside — to circle around it, to view it from different angles, to break it into parts. But each perspective is a static view. No matter how many perspectives we accumulate, we will never capture the **movement of the thing itself**, its internal duration, its creative becoming.

Bergson illustrates this with the example of a city:

> "Translate, analyze, develop — the object, far from being encompassed as the analysis advances, retreats before it. It was unique, and you thought you were progressively analyzing it because the multiplicity of the elements into which you divided it was growing. But it was all the time eluding your grasp, for these elements, which you thought were parts, were really so many different views of an indivisible whole."
> — Bergson, *Creative Mind*, "Introduction to Metaphysics"

Analysis substitutes the multiplicity of perspectives for the unity of the object. It replaces the thing with descriptions of the thing from various viewpoints. But the thing itself — its duration, its movement, its creative unfolding — can only be grasped by **intuition**.

### 3.3 Why Philosophy Has Been Trapped

The cinematographic illusion explains, for Bergson, why the history of philosophy has been trapped in a series of irresolvable dilemmas:

- **Mechanism vs. Finalism in biology**: Both share the same assumption — that the future is somehow given. For the mechanist, the future is determined by the past through law-like causal chains. For the finalist, the future is determined by an end or goal that acts as a cause. Both fail to recognize that the future is genuinely *created*, not pre-determined.

- **Realism vs. Idealism in epistemology**: Both assume that knowledge consists of representations. The realist says representations correspond to things; the idealist says representations constitute things. Neither asks whether knowledge might be something other than representation — whether it might be a **coincidence with the movement of the real**.

- **Determinism vs. Indeterminism in the free will debate**: Both accept the spatialization of time. The determinist says the future is already fixed; the indeterminist says it could go either way. Both miss the point: the future is not "fixed" or "open" in a spatial sense — it is being *created*, and creation is neither determined nor random.

Bergson's philosophy is, in this sense, an attempt to **step outside the dilemmas that arise from spatialized thinking** and to think from within duration itself.

## 4. Intuition vs. Intellect: Two Modes of Knowing

### 4.1 The Fundamental Distinction

The distinction between intuition and intellect is one of Bergson's most central and most misunderstood doctrines. It is crucial to understand that Bergson is **not** opposing rationality to irrationality, or knowledge to feeling. He is distinguishing two legitimate but fundamentally different modes of approaching reality, each suited to a different domain.

**The Intellect** is the faculty of analysis, calculation, and logical reasoning. It works by:
- Breaking wholes into parts
- Identifying stable elements and fixed laws
- Representing reality through concepts and symbols
- Projecting spatial categories onto temporal processes
- Focusing on the practical — what can be used, manipulated, and predicted

The intellect is perfectly adapted to the world of **matter** — the domain of things that are relatively stable, separate, and predictable. It is the tool of science and of practical action. As Bergson puts it, the intellect is "the faculty of manufacturing artificial objects, especially tools to make tools, and of indefinitely varying their manufacture."

**Intuition**, by contrast, is the faculty of **sympathetic coincidence with the object**. It is not a mystical feeling or an irrational hunch. It is a rigorous philosophical method that requires sustained intellectual effort:

> "Intuition ... is in the first place a consciousness, but a consciousness that has become disinterested and consequently dilated, that has been able to reflect upon its object and to expand it so far that it plunges into the depths of life and is able to follow the original, generative movement."
> — Bergson, *Creative Evolution*

### 4.2 Intuition as Philosophical Method

Bergson's most explicit statement of intuition as method appears in his 1903 article "Introduction to Metaphysics" (later reprinted in *The Creative Mind*). He offers three rules for the practice of intuition:

**Rule 1: State and create problems, don't merely solve pre-given ones.** Philosophical problems are not puzzles waiting to be solved; they are creations. The way we frame a problem determines the range of possible solutions. Many traditional philosophical problems (e.g., "How does the mind interact with the body?") are pseudo-problems generated by spatialized thinking. The task of philosophy is to formulate genuine problems — problems that emerge from duration itself.

**Rule 2: Discover the elements of each thing, not generic genera.** We must find the specific differences that make each thing what it is, not subsume it under general categories. Each thing has its own rhythm of duration, its own degree of contraction. We must enter into this specific rhythm, not impose abstract classifications.

**Rule 3: Grasp the thing from within, by intellectual sympathy.** This is the core of intuition: to transport oneself inside the object and to coincide with what is unique and therefore inexpressible in it. Not by feeling or empathy, but by an effort of the intelligence itself that reverses its habitual direction.

### 4.3 The Reversal of Thought

Bergson insists that intuition requires a **reversal of the habitual direction of thought**. Normally, the intellect moves from the complex to the simple, from the concrete to the abstract, from the living to the inert. Intuition moves in the opposite direction — toward the concrete, toward the living, toward the continuous.

This reversal is difficult because our natural habits of thought are shaped by practical needs and by the evolutionary function of the intellect:

> "Our intellect, in its legitimate though not essential ambition, naturally proceeds from the point of view of unity, taking up matter and attempting to reconstruct it as it is, by means of geometrical atomism."
> — Bergson, *Creative Mind*

We are trained — both biologically and culturally — to think discretely, spatially, and practically. To think intuitively, we must undo these habits. Bergson acknowledges that this is "unbearably difficult" — which is why so few philosophers have practiced it.

### 4.4 Intellect Does Not Equal Error

A crucial clarification: Bergson does not claim that the intellect produces error. The intellect is a legitimate cognitive faculty that produces genuine knowledge — specifically, knowledge of matter, of the relatively stable and spatialized aspects of reality. Science, built on intellect, gives us powerful and practically effective knowledge.

The problem arises when the intellect **oversteps its domain** — when it attempts to grasp life, consciousness, freedom, and creativity using the spatializing categories that work well for inert matter. The intellect is not wrong when it studies rocks; it is wrong (or rather, inadequate) when it treats consciousness as a thing, life as a mechanism, or time as a line.

> "Intellect is characterized by a natural inability to grasp life."
> — Bergson, *Creative Evolution*, Chapter 2

This is why philosophy, for Bergson, cannot be the handmaiden of science. Philosophy must use intuition to grasp what intellect cannot reach — not to contradict science, but to complement it, to think what science, by its methodological commitment to spatialization, necessarily leaves aside.

### 4.5 Bertrand Russell's Misreading

It is worth noting that Bertrand Russell famously misunderstood Bergson's doctrine of intuition. In his 1912 essay "The Philosophy of Bergson," Russell wrote:

> "The essence of instinct is that the subject knows without having the power to express his knowledge in words. ... The knowledge which Bergson attributes to intuition would seem to be of this kind."

Russell then argued that if intuition gives us knowledge that cannot be expressed, it cannot serve as a basis for philosophy, which requires communicable, verifiable claims. Bergsonians (most recently Leonard Lawlor) have responded that **intuition for Bergson is not ineffable in principle** — it becomes articulable through sustained philosophical effort. The initial intuitive grasp must then be translated into concepts, not to replace the intuition but to express it. The translation will never be perfect, but it is necessary and possible.

## 5. Memory and Matter

### 5.1 The Dualism of Matter and Memory

*Matter and Memory* (1896) is Bergson's most ambitious and arguably most difficult work. The book's subtitle — "Essay on the Relation of Body to Mind" — signals its purpose: to resolve the mind-body problem not by reducing one to the other (materialism or idealism) but by showing that both matter and memory are **degrees of contraction within duration**.

The book opens with a striking claim:

> "In theory, I can admit that all is matter. But in practice, I start from the data of consciousness, and from these data, matter can in no way be deduced."
> — Bergson, *Matter and Memory*, Introduction

Bergson's strategy is to begin from two irreducible givens: matter (as described by physics) and memory (as experienced in consciousness). He then shows that these are not two separate substances but two **extreme tendencies or "rhythms of duration."**

### 5.2 Pure Perception and the Image

Bergson begins by radically redefining perception. Against the representationalist tradition (which holds that we perceive internal representations of external objects), Bergson argues that **perception is virtual coincidence with the object**. In "pure perception" — a theoretical limit that never actually exists in experience — perception would be identical with the object itself.

> "In reality, pure perception, if it could be realized, would be a part of things with all the other parts."
> — Bergson, *Matter and Memory*, Chapter 1

Bergson defines matter as an "aggregate of images," where "image" means something between the "thing" of realism and the "representation" of idealism. An image exists in itself (not merely in a perceiving subject) but is also potentially perceived. Matter is the totality of these images, and perception is the selection, from this totality, of those images that are relevant to our possible actions.

This gives us a striking picture: **the body is not a thing among things but a center of action** — a privileged image that selects and organizes other images according to its practical interests. Perception is not the reception of data; it is the delimitation of a field of possible action.

### 5.3 Two Forms of Memory

Bergson's most famous contribution in this book is his distinction between **two fundamentally different forms of memory**:

**Habit-Memory (mémoire-habitude)**: This is memory inscribed in the body through repetition. When I learn to play a piece of music, my fingers "remember" the movements through habit. This type of memory is oriented toward action — it prepares the body to respond to similar situations in the future. It is mechanistic in the sense that it operates automatically, without conscious recollection.

**Pure Memory (mémoire pure)**: This is the preservation of every moment of our past in its unique, lived detail. Each experience, each perception, each feeling is conserved in its entirety. Pure memory is not oriented toward action; it is the survival of the past as past. It is spiritual rather than material, virtual rather than actual.

> "In reality, the past is conserved by itself, automatically. The past survives 'irresistibly.'"
> — Bergson, *Matter and Memory*, Chapter 3

This claim — that the past is preserved in its entirety — is one of Bergson's most radical. It is not a psychological thesis but an ontological one: the past is not gone; it is preserved as virtual reality, coexisting with the present.

### 5.4 The Cone of Memory

Bergson illustrates the relationship between pure memory and present action with his famous **cone diagram** (in *Matter and Memory*, Chapter 3):

```
          S (pure memory — the entirety of the past as virtual)
         /\
        /  \
       /    \
      /      \
     /        \
    /          \
   /            \
  AB (the present moment — the plane of action)
```

- **Point S** (the summit) represents pure memory in its totality — the entire contracted past as virtual presence.
- **Plane AB** (the base) represents the present plane of material reality and action.
- **The cone** represents the continuous process of memory contracting from the virtual totality of the past into the singular point of present action.
- **The sections** between S and AB represent different degrees of contraction — different ways the past can be actualized in the present.

In concrete experience, we never encounter pure memory at S (this would be total recollection, a dream-like state detached from action) or pure perception at AB (this would be pure responsiveness with no memory). Actual experience is always a **dynamic oscillation** between memory and perception, between virtual past and actual present.

### 5.5 The Body as Image and Center of Indeterminacy

For Bergson, the body is not a substance — it is an **image among images** with a key difference: it is the image that can *act*. The body is a "center of indetermination" within the flux of material images. Perception is the body's selection from surrounding images based on its possible actions; memory is the body's resource for enriching these actions with the accumulated past.

This reframes the mind-body problem entirely. There are not two substances (mind and body) that must somehow interact. There is a single reality — duration — that contracts at different rates. Matter is duration at its most relaxed, most repetitive, most nearly instantaneous. Mind is duration at its most contracted, most memory-laden, most tension-filled. The body occupies a middle position — a zone of interaction between the two rhythms.

> "Between matter itself, which we resolved into numberless vibrations, all linked together in uninterrupted continuity, all bound up with each other, and stretching forward in all directions without limit, and ourselves who concentrate virtually all of these vibrations into our perceptions, and expand, also virtually, our perceptions into images infinitely expanded, there is only a difference of degree, and not of kind."
> — Bergson, *Matter and Memory*, Chapter 5

This is one of the most radical claims in modern philosophy: **the difference between matter and mind is a difference of degree (of tension, of contraction, of rhythm), not a difference of kind**.

### 5.6 Recognition and the Two Movements

Bergson distinguishes two forms of recognition that mirror his two forms of memory:

**Automatic Recognition**: This is recognition by habit. When I recognize a familiar face, my body responds automatically — I prepare a greeting, my muscles tense, my expression shifts. This recognition is rooted in habit-memory and is oriented toward action. It is "automatic" because it bypasses conscious deliberation.

**Attentive Recognition**: This is recognition by conscious recollection. When I see an unfamiliar face that reminds me of someone, I search my memory, summoning images from the past. This recognition involves a back-and-forth movement between the present perception and the virtual past — movement up and down the cone of memory. It is "attentive" because it requires active engagement between memory and perception.

This theory of recognition has profound implications. It shows that perception is never "pure" — it is always already infused with memory. What we call "perception" is always a synthesis of the present and the past. This anticipates aspects of phenomenology (especially Merleau-Ponty's account of bodily perception) and contemporary cognitive science's emphasis on the role of memory in perception.

## 6. Creative Evolution and the Élan Vital

### 6.1 The Project of Creative Evolution

*Creative Evolution* (1907) is Bergson's magnum opus — the book that made him internationally famous and generated the most controversy. The book's ambition is enormous: to provide a philosophical account of life and evolution that is neither mechanistic nor finalist, neither deterministic nor teleological, but genuinely **creative**.

Bergson argues that both mechanism and finalism fail because they share a common assumption: **that the future is somehow given**. The mechanist says the future is determined by the past; the finalist says the future is determined by an end or purpose. Both deny genuine novelty — both reduce the future to a rearrangement of what already exists.

> "The mechanist sees only repetitions: all the originality of the future is resolved into a combination of elements already given. The finalist, for his part, admits only rearrangements: the future was already inscribed in a plan which was necessarily realized."
> — Bergson, *Creative Evolution*, Introduction

Bergson's alternative is to think evolution as **continuous creation** — as the production of genuinely novel forms that could not have been predicted from what preceded them.

### 6.2 The Élan Vital

The *élan vital* (vital impulse or vital force) is Bergson's name for the creative principle that drives evolution. It is neither a substance nor a force in the physical sense, but rather a **tendency, an impulse, a creative movement** that traverses matter and produces life.

Key features of the *élan vital*:

1. **It is not a pre-formed plan**: The *élan vital* does not have a blueprint or a goal. It creates as it goes, responding to obstacles and opportunities. Evolution is not the realization of a pre-existing design but an improvisation.

2. **It encounters resistance in matter**: Matter is the inverse movement of the *élan vital* — it is the tendency toward repetition, inertia, and entropy. Life must struggle against matter, and the forms that life takes are the result of this struggle. The branching paths of evolution represent different solutions to the problem of navigating material obstacles.

3. **It is discontinuous in its effects but continuous in its impulse**: The *élan vital* is one continuous movement, but it produces discrete species and forms. Each evolutionary line represents a different direction the impulse has taken, a different "choice" in response to material resistance.

4. **It is exhausted in the forms it produces**: Unlike a god who creates and remains transcendent, the *élan vital* is entirely immanent in its creations. It is the movement of creation itself, not a creator behind or above creation.

> "Life is the movement that matter makes possible. But the living being takes what it can from matter, and the rest it creates. Evolution is creative because each new form that emerges is genuinely novel."
> — paraphrase of Bergson, *Creative Evolution*, Chapter 2

### 6.3 The Four Moments of Evolution

Bergson describes evolution as proceeding through four major moments:

**I. Vegetative Life**: The most basic form of life, characterized by the capacity to absorb matter from the environment and transform it. Vegetative life is relatively unconscious — it is the *élan vital* in its most elemental form, a blind tendency to organize and reproduce.

**II. Instinct**: The next stage is the development of instinct — the capacity to act in complex, species-specific ways without deliberation. Instinct is "sympathy" — the capacity to identify with the object of action. The bee that builds a hexagonal honeycomb "knows" what it is doing through instinctual sympathy with its own body and environment, not through intellectual calculation.

**III. Intelligence**: The most distinctive feature of human beings (and to some extent other animals) is intelligence — the capacity to make tools, to reason, to abstract, to manufacture. Intelligence is the *élan vital*'s most divergent turn — the direction in which life becomes capable of stepping back from its own activity and representing it to itself. Intelligence is suited to manipulating inert matter, not to grasping life itself.

**IV. Intuition**: The culmination of evolution is not intelligence but intuition — the capacity to reverse the intellectual habit and to enter into sympathy with life itself. Intuition is what intelligence can do when it turns back upon itself, when it becomes capable of grasping its own movement. It is a "super-intelligence" that encompasses and exceeds the spatializing intellect.

> "An evolution of thought, that is, an expansion of consciousness, is possible. Intelligence, which was the light generated by the cooling of a certain number of points, is being transformed into a center of incandescence, from which rays of light would be sent out in all directions."
> — Bergson, *Creative Evolution*, Conclusion

### 6.4 Instinct vs. Intelligence

Bergson's treatment of instinct and intelligence is one of the most original contributions of *Creative Evolution*. He argues that instinct and intelligence are two divergent directions of the same vital impulse:

- **Instinct** works from within; it is organic knowledge, knowledge by sympathy, knowledge that is embedded in the body.
- **Intelligence** works from without; it is geometrical knowledge, knowledge by analysis, knowledge that represents objects from a distance.
- Instinct is perfected in bees and ants (social insects with extraordinary specialized abilities).
- Intelligence reaches its peak in humans (tool-making, language, science).

Neither is inherently superior; both are adaptations. But intelligence has the unique capacity to become **conscious of itself** — to recognize its own limitations and to seek out the intuitive complement it lacks. This self-conscious intelligence is philosophy.

### 6.5 Negation and the Nature of Nothingness

One of the most philosophically significant arguments in *Creative Evolution* is Bergson's critique of the concept of **nothingness** and **negation**. Bergson argues that the idea of "nothing" is a pseudo-idea — it is not genuinely conceivable.

The reasoning goes like this: when we try to think "nothing," we actually think of something — often the disappearance or annihilation of what is. But this presupposes what is. The idea of nothing is a retrospective illusion generated by the intellect's ability to subtract: we start with something and subtract its properties until we arrive at "nothing."

This critique of negation has enormous implications. It undermines:

- The argument from contingency (why is there something rather than nothing?)
- Dialectical thinking in the Hegelian tradition (which makes negation the motor of development)
- Theological arguments that depend on the contrast between being and non-being

Bergson writes: "The idea of 'nothing' ... is a pseudo-idea. ... There is not more, there is even less, in the idea of 'the absence of everything' than there is in the idea of 'everything that exists.'"

This critique is developed through a detailed genealogy of the idea of nothingness, showing how it arises from a confusion between two operations: the perception of the absence of something within a given context, and the intellectual projection of this absence onto the world as a whole.

### 6.6 Order, Disorder, and the Illusions of Negation

Extending the critique of negation, Bergson analyzes how disorder is merely the order we expected but did not find, and nothing is the something present with the something expected subtracted. These are not positive realities but retrospective intellectual constructions. The intellect projects its disappointment or surprise as objective features of reality.

This analysis has significant implications for philosophy of science and theories of information. Bergson anticipates later critiques (including information theory) that show "disorder" is not an objective state but a mismatch between an expectation and reality.

## 7. Laughter: The Mechanical Encrusted on the Living

### 7.1 The Comic as Philosophical Method

Bergson's essay *Laughter: An Essay on the Meaning of the Comic* (1900) is often overlooked in discussions of his philosophy, but it provides a crucial window into his broader philosophical project. The essay's central thesis is elegantly simple:

> "Laughter is the reaction to 'something mechanical encrusted on the living.'"
> — Bergson, *Laughter*, Chapter 1

### 7.2 The Mechanism of the Comic

Bergson argues that comedy arises whenever we perceive **rigidity, repetition, or mechanism in a context where we expect flexibility, spontaneity, or life**. Examples include:

- A person slipping on a banana peel (rigidity of body where flexibility was expected)
- The absent-minded professor (fixation of mind on abstract thoughts while practical situation demands attention)
- The bureaucrat following rules mindlessly (institutional mechanism replacing human judgment)
- The character with an obsessive tic or catchphrase (repetition substituting for spontaneity)

The underlying pattern is always the same: **life demands suppleness, and we encounter rigidity**. Laughter is the social response to this incongruity — a corrective mechanism that punishes inflexibility and encourages adaptability.

### 7.3 Laughter as Social Sanction

Bergson emphasizes that laughter has a social function. We rarely laugh alone; laughter typically occurs in groups. Its purpose is to **correct deviance from social norms** — specifically, the norm of flexibility, attentiveness, and adaptability to the flow of life. When someone behaves mechanically, society laughs them back into awareness.

> "Laughter must answer to certain requirements of life in common. It must have a *social* signification."
> — Bergson, *Laughter*

This analysis reveals something profound about Bergson's view of life: life is fundamentally **creative, unpredictable, and adaptive**. Anything that substitutes rigidity for flexibility, repetition for creation, mechanism for life is not just wrong — it is *comical*. Laughter is the universe's way of saying, "Wake up! You've become a thing."

### 7.4 Connection to the Broader Philosophy

The theory of laughter illuminates Bergson's entire philosophical project. The comic reveals the absurdity of treating living processes as if they were substances, of substituting rigid categories for fluid reality, of replacing duration with spatialized positions. It is, in miniature, the same critique that Bergson levels against the entire philosophical tradition.

The comic, for Bergson, is a philosophical method in its own right — a mode of perception that reveals the gap between reality and our representations of it. Laughter is, in a sense, the body's intuitive recognition that the intellect has gone astray.

## 8. The Two Sources of Morality and Religion

### 8.1 The Final Major Work

Bergson's last major book, *The Two Sources of Morality and Religion* (1932), extends his vitalist philosophy into the domains of ethics, politics, and religion. It is often the least studied of his works, but it contains profound insights into the nature of social life and the ultimate direction of evolution.

### 8.2 Two Types of Morality

Bergson distinguishes two sources or types of morality:

**Closed Morality**: The morality of a particular society, group, or tribe. It is based on obligation, pressure, and habit. Its function is to ensure social cohesion and survival. It is "closed" because it applies only to members of the group and excludes outsiders. Closed morality is rooted in the tendency toward social solidarity that nature implants in living beings through evolution.

Bergson argues that our experience of moral obligation — the "thou shalt" of duty — is not a rational command but a social pressure, analogous to natural instinct. When we feel we "must" follow a moral rule, what we are experiencing is the weight of the closed society, the accumulated force of social habituation.

**Open Morality**: The morality that transcends particular groups and embraces all of humanity. It is based on aspiration rather than obligation, on creative love rather than social pressure. It is "open" because it is not restricted to any particular society. Open morality is the creation of exceptional individuals — moral heroes and saints — who break through the boundaries of closed morality and extend love to all.

### 8.3 Static vs. Dynamic Religion

Corresponding to the two types of morality are two types of religion:

**Static Religion** is the religion of the closed society. Its function is to provide social cohesion, to protect the individual against the paralyzing awareness of his own mortality, and to reinforce social norms through myth. Static religion is essentially a defensive mechanism — it protects society against the individual's potential for anti-social behavior (enabled by intelligence) and against the individual's potential for self-destructive despair (also enabled by intelligence).

**Dynamic Religion** is the religion of the open society. It is based on direct contact or communion with the creative impulse of life — what Bergson calls the "principle at the root of all religion." Dynamic religion is not institutional or dogmatic; it is experiential and creative. Bergson identifies its highest form as **mysticism** — the direct experience of union with the creative force of the universe.

### 8.4 The Mystic and the Creation of the Open Society

Bergson's treatment of mysticism is one of the most distinctive features of *The Two Sources*. The mystic, for Bergson, is not an escapist or a contemplative withdrawer from the world. The mystic is the **agent of creative evolution at the human level**.

The mystic experiences an ecstatic union with the creative impulse (*élan vital*) and is driven to act — to spread love, justice, and creative transformation in the world. Bergson cites Christian mystics (especially St. Teresa of Ávila and St. Paul) as paradigmatic examples, but the concept is not limited to any particular tradition.

> "The mystic ... achieves a state of contact, and consequently of partial coincidence, with the creative effort which life itself manifests."
> — Bergson, *The Two Sources*, Chapter 3

The mystic's joy is creative joy — the joy of participating in the ongoing creation of the universe. This joy is not passive contemplation; it is "a call, a will, an appeal for action." The mystic does not rest in contemplation but is driven to love, to create, to transform.

### 8.5 The Creative Emotion

What drives the mystic and, by extension, the open society is not reason or deliberation but **creative emotion** — a transformative affectivity that destabilizes the habitual self and opens the individual to new possibilities. Bergson compares creative emotions to "unstable mental states" but emphasizes that genuine creative emotion must result in action, not mere contemplation.

Bergson explicitly critiques Kantian moral philosophy on this point. Kant derives morality from pure practical reason — from the universal form of the moral law. Bergson argues that this is a "psychological error": the formulas of reason cannot produce the force of moral action. What drives genuine moral transformation is emotional creativity — the impetus of love — not rational calculation.

"The impetus of love ... is a creative emotion." The mystic explicates this emotion into actions and representations, which can then be further explicated into formulas — but the formulas are the *expression* of creation, not its *source*. As Bergson says, "Re-establish the duality, and the difficulties vanish."

### 8.6 The Open Society and the Democracy to Come

The culmination of Bergson's philosophical vision is the idea of the **open society** — a society that is not closed by tribal, national, or ideological boundaries, but open to all of humanity and, ultimately, to all of life. The open society is not a given; it is a creative achievement, driven by the moral heroes and mystics who break through the walls of the closed society.

This concept of the open society has had profound influence on subsequent political philosophy, particularly through its adoption by Karl Popper (though Popper's usage differs significantly from Bergson's) and, more recently, through its influence on Deleuze and Jacques Derrida's concept of "the democracy to come."

For Deleuze, the open society connects to his concept of multiplicity: a genuine community that is not defined by a proper name or a unifying identity. For Derrida, the "democracy to come" always remains in need of a name — it cannot be finalized or achieved, because it is essentially open, unfinished, and creative.

## 9. Bergson's Influence and Legacy

### 9.1 Immediate Influence

During his lifetime, Bergson's influence extended far beyond academic philosophy:

- **William James**: The American pragmatist was an enthusiastic advocate of Bergson's work, writing that reading Bergson made him feel as if "we were no longer mere parochial earthlings, but citizens of the universe." James's own concept of the "stream of consciousness" was deeply influenced by Bergson's *durée*.

- **Alfred North Whitehead**: Whitehead's process philosophy, with its emphasis on events rather than substances, bears clear affinities with Bergson's thought, though Whitehead never fully acknowledged the debt.

- **Marcel Proust**: The famous novelist's exploration of involuntary memory in *In Search of Lost Time* is deeply Bergsonian. The famous madeleine scene exemplifies the recovery and expansion of the past through pure memory.

- **Cubism and Modernist Art**: Bergson's critique of spatialized time and his emphasis on duration influenced the Cubist movement (the 1903 "Introduction to Metaphysics" predates the first Cubist exhibition). The attempt to represent movement and duration on a static canvas reflects Bergsonian concerns.

- **T.S. Eliot**: The poet attended Bergson's lectures in Paris in 1910–1911 and wrote about Bergsonian philosophy in his graduate studies.

### 9.2 The Eclipse of Bergsonism

After World War II, Bergson's influence declined dramatically, for several reasons:

1. **The rise of phenomenology and existentialism**: Husserl, Heidegger, Sartre, and Merleau-Ponty dominated post-war French philosophy, displacing Bergson from the center of intellectual life. Although Sartre and Merleau-Ponty explicitly acknowledged their debt to Bergson, the new generation defined itself against him.

2. **The linguistic turn**: Philosophers like Derrida embraced Heidegger's dictum that "language is the house of being," while Bergson's deep suspicion of language (as inherently spatializing and symbolic) seemed problematic in an era that took language as the primary philosophical datum.

3. **The mysticism of *The Two Sources***: The religious and mystical dimensions of Bergson's later work did not harmonize with the secular, scientific aspirations of post-war Continental philosophy.

4. **Political associations**: During the interwar period, Bergson's philosophy was associated with anti-intellectualism, irrationalism, and even proto-fascism by some critics, though these characterizations are historically inaccurate and philosophically unfair.

### 9.3 Deleuze and the Revival of Bergsonism

The decisive turning point in the reception of Bergson was Gilles Deleuze's book *Bergsonism* (1966). Deleuze's reading of Bergson is transformative:

- **Multiplicity as the central concept**: Deleuze identifies Bergson's concept of multiplicity — unifying heterogeneity and continuity — as his most revolutionary contribution, a concept that anticipates Deleuze's own ontology of difference.

- **The virtual and the actual**: Deleuze develops Bergson's distinction between the virtual and the actual into a central pillar of his own philosophy. The virtual is not the possible; it is the real that is not yet actualized. Difference, for Deleuze, is primarily virtual difference — the creative reservoir from which actual things emerge.

- **Duration as the model for becoming**: All of Deleuze's "becomings" (becoming-woman, becoming-animal, becoming-imperceptible) are modeled on Bergson's concept of duration as continuous, heterogeneous, creative flow.

- **Critique of negation**: Bergson's critique of negation in *Creative Evolution* becomes a key resource for Deleuze's anti-Hegelianism — his rejection of the negative as the motor of dialectical development.

### 9.4 Bergson and Phenomenology

Although Bergson and phenomenology (especially Husserl's) have been treated as separate traditions, scholars have increasingly recognized significant convergences:

- **Merleau-Ponty** acknowledged Bergson as a forerunner, particularly in the areas of bodily perception and the critique of objectivism. Merleau-Ponty's concept of "flesh" (chair) as a pre-reflective ontological tissue shares structural similarities with Bergson's concept of duration.

- **Emmanuel Lévinas** was deeply influenced by Bergson, particularly by the concepts of duration and of the open society. Lévinas's critique of ontology as a "philosophy of the Same" resonates with Bergson's critique of the intellect's tendency to reduce difference to identity.

- **Recent scholarship** (Kelly 2010, Müller 2017, Lefebvre & Schott 2020) has explored the connections between Bergson and phenomenology more systematically, showing that Bergson's "Introduction to Metaphysics" shares deep methodological affinities with phenomenological reduction.

### 9.5 Bergson and Pragmatism

Bergson's relationship with American pragmatism, particularly with William James, is one of the most significant intellectual friendships of the early 20th century. James read Bergson's "Introduction to Metaphysics" (1903) with enormous enthusiasm, writing:

> "Bergson's ideas ... give one something like the effect of looking out from a lofty peak over a vast landscape of thought."

Key affinities between Bergson and pragmatism include:

- **Anti-foundationalism**: Both reject the search for certain foundations of knowledge.
- **Temporal realism**: Both take time seriously as a genuine dimension of reality, not a mere framework.
- **Practical orientation**: Both philosophy as connected to life, action, and experience, not mere abstract reasoning.
- **Pluralism**: Both reject monistic systems in favor of pluralistic, open-ended approaches to reality.

The main difference is one of emphasis: pragmatism tends to focus on the practical consequences of ideas, while Bergson is more concerned with the metaphysical structure of reality. But these differences are complementary rather than antagonistic.

### 9.6 Contemporary Bergsonism

The current revival of Bergsonian scholarship — sometimes called "new Bergsonism" — takes several forms:

1. **Deleuzian readings**: Continuing the work of Deleuze, scholars explore Bergson's concepts of multiplicity, the virtual, and difference.

2. **Phenomenological readings**: Scholars like Leonard Lawlor and Keith Ansell Pearson reconnect Bergson with the phenomenological tradition.

3. **Critical and political readings**: Recent work (Pitts & Westmoreland 2019, *Beyond Bergson*) explores Bergson's relationship to race, gender, and colonialism — questions that were largely absent from earlier Bergsonian scholarship.

4. **Vitalist and new materialist readings**: Bergson's concepts of the *élan vital* and of matter as tension are being reinterpreted in light of contemporary debates in vitalism and new materialism (Ansell Pearson 2002, Bennett 2010).

5. **Temporal and process philosophy**: Bergson remains a central figure in the ongoing development of process philosophy, particularly in debates about the nature of time, creativity, and becoming.

6. **Scientific engagements**: Scholars like Suzanne Guerlac have explored Bergson's relationship with the sciences — including his complex engagement with Einstein's relativity theory — showing that Bergson was not anti-science but rather sought to complement scientific knowledge with philosophical intuition.

## 10. Conclusion: The Enduring Relevance of Bergsonism

Henri Bergson's philosophy offers a radical and coherent alternative to the dominant tendencies of modern Western thought. His critique of spatialized time, his defense of intuition as a philosophical method, his ontology of creative becoming, and his vision of the open society together constitute a philosophical project of extraordinary depth and breadth.

At its core, Bergsonism is a **philosophy of life** — not in the biological sense, but in the sense that it takes life (understood as creative becoming) as the model for understanding reality. Against the mechanistic, deterministic, and spatializing tendencies of modern science and philosophy, Bergson insists on the irreducibility of duration, the creativity of evolution, the power of intuition, and the openness of the future.

Bergson's relevance today is perhaps greater than ever. In an age when artificial intelligence threatens to reduce thought to computation, when algorithmic systems increasingly model human behavior as patterns of data, when the future appears either predetermined by climate catastrophe or by techno-capitalist logic — Bergson's insistence on the **irreducible creativity of life, the genuine openness of the future, and the necessity of intuition** offers a vital corrective.

> "To exist is to change, to change is to mature, to mature is to go on creating oneself endlessly."
> — Bergson, *Creative Evolution*

Bergson's philosophy is not a system — it is a movement, an orientation, an invitation to think differently, to reverse the habitual direction of thought, to enter into sympathy with the creative movement of life itself. In this sense, Bergson remains, as Deleuze called him, "one of our greatest philosophers" — not because he gave us answers, but because he showed us how to ask better questions.
