# Crypto Is to Agents What Property Rights Were to Capitalism

_Seed for a longer piece. March 2, 2026._

---

## The One Liner

**Crypto is to agents what property rights were to capitalism.** Not the energy source. Not the machines. The legal fiction that made the whole thing work. The thing that turned raw capability into an economy.

## Why It Works

Three things at once:

1. **Positions crypto as foundational, not speculative.** This isn't about price. It's about structure.
2. **Makes agents the protagonist.** Not humans trading jpegs. Autonomous entities that need economic agency.
3. **Borrows credibility from something everyone accepts.** Nobody argues against property rights. The analogy carries that weight.

## The Argument

Agents need to transact. They need identity. They need to hold value, make payments, prove ownership, coordinate with other agents without a human cosigning every interaction.

Traditional finance has no concept of a non human economic actor. Try opening a bank account for a Claude instance. Crypto rails give agents economic agency: wallets they can hold, transactions they can sign, protocols they can interact with programmatically. No KYC for a smart contract call. No business hours. No wire transfer delays. The only financial infrastructure that treats an API call and a human click as equivalent.

Without property rights, markets don't function regardless of how much energy you have. Without crypto, agents can think but they can't _do business_.

## Rejected Metaphors

- **"Crypto is the oil of the agentic age"**: Oil is consumable fuel. Crypto is infrastructure. Close but not precise enough.
- **"Crypto is the electrical grid"**: Loops back on itself because crypto literally runs on the electrical grid. Confusing.
- **"Crypto is the internet of the agentic age"**: Too vague. The internet is a communication layer. Crypto is an ownership layer.

## The Sidestep

The framing sidesteps the usual crypto debate entirely. You're not defending the technology. You're describing a structural requirement. Agents need to own, transact, and coordinate. That's not a crypto opinion. That's an architecture problem. Crypto just happens to be the only solution that exists.

## Threads to Pull

- What does "agent property rights" actually look like in practice? Wallet ownership, NFTs as credentials, token gated access?
- If crypto is property rights, what's the equivalent of "rule of law" that enforces them? Smart contracts? Protocol governance?
- Historical parallels: how did property rights emerge in early capitalism? Was there a similar period of skepticism?
- The agent economy doesn't need all of crypto. Which parts matter? (Wallets, signing, programmable transactions. Probably not DeFi yield farming.)
- When does an agent's economic activity require human oversight vs full autonomy? Where's the line?
