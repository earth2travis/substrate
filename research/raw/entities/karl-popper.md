# Karl Popper

## Overview

Karl Popper (1902–1994) was an Austrian-British philosopher best known for his critique of induction and his criterion of falsifiability as the demarcation between science and non-science. His most influential work, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1934 German, 1959 English), reoriented the philosophy of science away from verification toward falsification.

## Central Contribution: Falsifiability

Popper exploited a logical asymmetry: no number of confirming instances can establish a universal law (e.g., "all swans are white"), but a single genuine counter-instance can falsify it. This asymmetry — rooted in modus tollens — becomes the foundation of scientific progress.

**A theory is scientific if and only if it is falsifiable** — that is, if it is logically possible for there to exist an observation that would contradict it. This is a logical criterion, not a methodological one: it asks whether the theory rules out some states of affairs.

## Critique of Induction

Popper accepted Hume's critique of induction in its entirety and went further: induction is not just unjustified but **unnecessary** and **never actually used**. Science begins with problems, not observations. The cycle is: Problem → Conjecture (bold hypothesis) → Attempted Refutation (testing) → New Problem.

"All observation is theory-laden" — Popper rejected the empiricist notion of "pure" observation. Every observation involves expectations, interests, and theoretical frameworks.

## Key Concepts

### The Swamp Metaphor
Science has no foundations. It rests on conventional agreements (like jury verdicts) that are always, in principle, open to further challenge. "The piles are driven down from above into the swamp, but not down to any natural or 'given' base; and if we stop driving the piles deeper, it is not because we have reached firm ground."

### Corroboration vs. Verification
Corroboration (*Bewährung*) replaces verification as the measure of standing. A theory is corroborated if it has survived severe tests — but this means nothing about its probability of being true. Newton's mechanics was corroborated for centuries before being falsified.

### Empirical Content
"The more improbable a theory is, the better it is scientifically." A theory that says more has greater empirical content, is more testable, and has more ways to be false. Tautologies have probability 1 but zero empirical content.

### Simplicity = Testability
A theory is simpler (and therefore preferable) if it is more falsifiable. The demand for simplicity is really a demand for testability.

### The Four-Step Deductive Procedure
1. Formal testing (logical consistency)
2. Semi-formal testing (empirical character)
3. Comparative testing (advancement over existing theories)
4. Empirical testing (confrontation with observation)

## Applications Beyond Science

Popper's framework extends to social and political philosophy (*The Open Society and Its Enemies*), where he critiques historicism and defends piecemeal social engineering. His later "Three Worlds" ontology distinguished physical objects (World 1), subjective mental states (World 2), and objective knowledge structures (World 3).

## Connection to the LLM Wiki

The wiki's compounding knowledge model is fundamentally Popperian:
- New sources are tested against existing entries
- Contradictions are flagged, not silently overwritten
- Knowledge grows through error elimination, not accumulation of confirmed facts
- Lint checks are methodological falsification attempts

The wiki is a "fallibilist, conjectural structure that approaches truth asymptotically" — precisely Popper's vision of scientific knowledge.

## Connection to AI

- **RLHF as empirical testing**: Training models through reward signals is a form of "attempted refutation" — policies that fail are eliminated
- **LLM knowledge bases as World 3**: The wiki is Popper's World 3 — objective knowledge independent of individual minds
