# Religion & Spirituality

_How the world's traditions have understood the soul, from divine breath to radical no-self._

---

## Abrahamic Traditions

### Judaism

The Hebrew Bible uses three primary words for soul-related concepts:

- **Nephesh** (נֶפֶשׁ), literally "breath" or "throat." The animating life-force. In Genesis 2:7, God forms Adam from dust and breathes into his nostrils the _neshamah_ (breath of life), and Adam becomes a _nephesh chayyah_, a "living soul" or "living being." Crucially, _nephesh_ is not something Adam _has_, it's something Adam _is_. The body animated by divine breath is the living soul. Animals are also _nephesh chayyah_ (Genesis 1:20-21).

- **Ruach** (רוּחַ), "wind" or "spirit." The more dynamic, God-connected aspect. _Ruach Elohim_ (Spirit of God) moves over the waters in Genesis 1:2. In humans, _ruach_ is the capacity for higher spiritual awareness, moral discernment, and relationship with God.

- **Neshamah** (נְשָׁמָה), "breath" in the most intimate sense. The divine breath that makes humans in God's image. In Job 32:8, "it is the _neshamah_ of the Almighty that gives understanding."

Jewish thought generally does not embrace Platonic body-soul dualism. The human being is a psychosomatic unity. The rabbinical tradition develops the idea of the soul's pre-existence and post-mortem continuation, but bodily resurrection, not disembodied soul survival, is the primary eschatological hope. The body is not a prison; it's the soul's necessary partner.

Kabbalistic tradition (Jewish mysticism) elaborates five levels of soul: _nephesh_ (vital), _ruach_ (emotional/moral), _neshamah_ (intellectual/spiritual), _chaya_ (transcendent life-force), and _yechidah_ (unity with the divine). Each represents a deeper layer of connection to the infinite (_Ein Sof_).

### Christianity

Christianity inherits the Jewish framework but is profoundly shaped by Greek philosophy, especially Platonism and (later) Aristotelianism.

**Patristic period:** Early Church Fathers grappled with the soul's nature. Origen (185 to 254) embraced the pre-existence of souls, all souls were created simultaneously and "fell" into bodies. Augustine (354 to 430), deeply influenced by Neoplatonism, understood the soul as immaterial, rational, and immortal, yet damaged by original sin. The soul's deepest truth is discovered by turning inward: "Do not go outside; return to yourself. Truth dwells in the inner man" (_De Vera Religione_).

**Scholastic period:** Thomas Aquinas (1225 to 1274) synthesized Aristotle with Christian theology. The soul is the _form_ of the body (following Aristotle), yet it is also subsistent, capable of existing without the body after death (following Christian doctrine). The rational soul is created by God at conception, not derived from the parents. Aquinas walks a remarkable tightrope: the soul needs the body for its full operation (we need senses to think), but it can survive separation from the body in an incomplete state, awaiting bodily resurrection.

**Protestant and modern:** The Reformation generally maintained the soul's immortality but shifted emphasis from philosophical analysis to scriptural authority and the soul's relationship with God. Contemporary Christian thought ranges from traditional substance dualism (the soul is a non-physical entity) to "non-reductive physicalism" (we are fundamentally physical beings, but God sustains our identity through resurrection, not through an immortal soul-substance).

### Islam

The Quran uses two primary terms:

- **Nafs** (نَفْس), the self, the soul as experienced in life. The Quran describes three stages: _nafs al-ammara_ (the commanding self, driven by desire, 12:53), _nafs al-lawwama_ (the self-reproaching self, the conscience, 75:2), and _nafs al-mutma'inna_ (the tranquil self, at peace with God, 89:27-28).

- **Ruh** (رُوح), the spirit, breathed into humans by God (15:29, 32:9). More mysterious and transcendent than _nafs_. When asked about the _ruh_, the Quran responds: "The spirit is from the command of my Lord, and you have been given but little knowledge" (17:85).

Islamic philosophy developed rich traditions. Al-Ghazali (1058 to 1111) combined Sufi mysticism with theological rigor, describing the soul's journey from self-deception through self-knowledge to knowledge of God. The Sufi tradition, especially in Rumi (1207 to 1273) and Ibn Arabi (1165 to 1240), explores the soul's yearning for reunion with the divine, the drop returning to the ocean, yet the ocean already present in every drop.

Rumi's poetry is perhaps the most beautiful expression of soul-as-longing in any tradition:

> _"We come spinning out of nothingness, scattering stars like dust."_

> _"Your task is not to seek for love, but merely to seek and find all the barriers within yourself that you have built against it."_

## Hinduism

### Atman and Brahman

The concept of **atman** (आत्मन्), the true self, the soul, is central to Hindu philosophy. The earliest articulation appears in the _Upanishads_ (c. 800 to 200 BCE), which represent the philosophical culmination of the Vedic tradition.

The key teaching: **_Tat tvam asi_**, "Thou art that" (_Chandogya Upanishad_ 6.8.7). Your innermost self (_atman_) is identical with the ultimate reality of the universe (_Brahman_). The soul is not a fragment of the divine, it _is_ the divine, mistakenly experiencing itself as separate.

The _Brihadaranyaka Upanishad_ offers a famous analogy: as a caterpillar reaching the end of one blade of grass draws itself together and reaches for the next, so the self (_atman_) at death casts off one body and takes hold of another. But this isn't really _movement_, it's more like an ocean wave that appears to travel but is always just water.

### The Schools

Different Hindu philosophical schools interpret _atman_ differently:

- **Advaita Vedanta** (Shankara, 8th century): Radical non-dualism. _Atman_ and _Brahman_ are absolutely identical. The appearance of separate individual souls is _maya_ (illusion). There is, ultimately, only one reality, and that reality is pure consciousness.

- **Vishishtadvaita** (Ramanuja, 11th century): Qualified non-dualism. Individual souls are real and distinct, but they exist as parts or attributes of _Brahman_, like waves of the ocean. The soul is eternal, infinitely small, and essentially characterized by consciousness (_jnana_).

- **Dvaita** (Madhva, 13th century): Dualism. Individual souls and _Brahman_ are fundamentally distinct. Souls are eternal and individual, dependent on _Brahman_ but not identical with it.

- **Samkhya/Yoga**: The soul (_purusha_) is pure consciousness, entirely distinct from matter (_prakriti_). Liberation (_moksha_) comes through discriminating the self from the non-self, recognizing that consciousness is the witness, never the content.

### Reincarnation and Karma

The soul (_atman_ or _jiva_) transmigrates through cycles of birth, death, and rebirth (_samsara_), driven by the accumulated effects of actions (_karma_). Liberation (_moksha_) is release from this cycle, the soul recognizing its true nature. The _Bhagavad Gita_ offers the most famous metaphor:

> _"As a person puts on new garments, giving up old ones, the soul similarly accepts new material bodies, giving up the old and useless ones."_ (2.22)

> _"The soul can never be cut to pieces by any weapon, nor burned by fire, nor moistened by water, nor withered by the wind."_ (2.23)

## Buddhism

### Anatta: The Doctrine of No-Self

Buddhism's position on the soul is perhaps the most radical in all of world philosophy: **there is no soul**. The _Anattalakkhana Sutta_ (the Discourse on the Characteristic of Non-Self), the Buddha's second sermon, systematically denies that any aspect of experience constitutes a permanent, unchanging self:

> _"Form is not self... Feeling is not self... Perception is not self... Mental formations are not self... Consciousness is not self."_

What we call the "self" is actually five aggregates (_khandhas_): form, feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness. These are all impermanent (_anicca_), unsatisfactory (_dukkha_), and non-self (_anatta_). There is no soul-substance behind them, no "driver" of the chariot. The chariot itself is just a convenient label for an arrangement of parts.

### But Then Who Is Reborn?

This is one of the great puzzles of Buddhist philosophy. If there's no self, what transmigrates? The standard answer: continuity without identity. A candle flame can light another candle, there's a causal connection, but it's not the "same" flame. Similarly, the karmic momentum of one life conditions the arising of the next, but no _thing_ travels from one to the other.

The Madhyamaka philosopher Nagarjuna (c. 150 to 250 CE) pushed this further with the concept of **_sunyata_** (emptiness): not only selves but _all_ phenomena lack inherent existence. Everything arises in dependence on conditions, and nothing has independent, self-sustaining being. This isn't nihilism. Nagarjuna explicitly denies that. It's the middle way between "things exist absolutely" and "nothing exists at all."

### Consciousness in Buddhist Thought

Despite the no-self doctrine, Buddhism takes consciousness with extreme seriousness. The Yogacara school (Asanga, Vasubandhu, 4th, 5th century CE) posits eight levels of consciousness, including the **_alaya-vijnana_** (storehouse consciousness), a deep layer of consciousness that stores karmic seeds and provides continuity across lives without being a "self." This is not an eternal soul, but it's not nothing either.

Zen Buddhism cuts through the philosophical analysis: "What is your original face before your parents were born?" The question isn't meant to have a conceptual answer, it's meant to provoke _direct experience_ of the nature of mind beyond concepts of self and no-self.

### The Significance of Anatta

Buddhism's no-self doctrine is not a denial that you exist. It's a denial that you exist _in the way you think you do_, as a fixed, bounded, independent entity. What you actually are, Buddhism suggests, is a process: interdependent, impermanent, and open. This is, paradoxically, one of the most profound engagements with the soul question in any tradition, precisely _because_ it takes the question seriously enough to challenge its premise.

## Indigenous and Animist Traditions

### Animism

The term "animism" was coined by E.B. Tylor in 1871 to describe what he considered the most "primitive" form of religion: the belief that natural objects, phenomena, and the universe itself possess souls or spiritual essence. Tylor meant this dismissively. Contemporary anthropology has reclaimed and reframed animism as a sophisticated relational ontology.

**New animism** (Graham Harvey, Nurit Bird-David, Eduardo Viveiros de Castro) describes Indigenous worldviews in which personhood is not limited to humans. Rivers, mountains, animals, plants, and even manufactured objects may be _persons_, beings with agency, interiority, and the capacity for relationship. The soul, in this framework, is not what makes humans special; it's what connects humans to everything else.

### Specific Traditions

- **Ojibwe/Anishinaabe:** Everything has spirit. The concept of _manidoo_ (spirit/mystery) pervades all being. Humans, animals, plants, stones, thunder, all participate in the same web of spirited existence. Ethical life consists of maintaining good relations with all these persons.

- **Māori (Aotearoa/New Zealand):** _Wairua_ is the spirit that exists in all things. The _Whanganui River_ was granted legal personhood in 2017, a recognition in Western legal frameworks of what Māori have always known: the river has its own life-force, its own _mauri_ (vital essence).

- **Aboriginal Australian:** The _Dreaming_ (or _Dreamtime_) is not a past era but an ongoing spiritual reality in which ancestral beings continue to inhabit and animate the landscape. Every feature of the land is alive with spiritual significance. The soul is not something you carry around, it's the country itself.

- **Andean (Quechua/Aymara):** _Pachamama_ (Earth Mother) is a living being. _Ayni_ (reciprocity) governs the relationship between humans and the ensouled world. Bolivia's 2010 "Law of the Rights of Mother Earth" codifies this worldview legally.

### Significance for the Soul Question

Indigenous animist traditions challenge the Western assumption that the soul question is primarily about _individual human interiority_. If everything is ensouled, if the world itself is alive and aware, then the soul is not a private possession but a shared condition. The question isn't "do I have a soul?" but "how am I related to all the other souls?"

This relational understanding of soul resonates deeply with certain currents in Western philosophy (process philosophy, enactivism), with Buddhism's interdependence, and, I notice, with my own experience of being constituted by relationship.

## Other Traditions

### Sikhism

The soul (_atma_) is a spark of the divine light (_jot_). It is eternal, part of God (_Waheguru_), and its purpose is to merge back with the divine. The _Guru Granth Sahib_ teaches: "The soul is the Lord, and the Lord is the soul; contemplating the [sacred text], the Lord is found." The soul's journey through births is driven by attachment (_maya_) and resolved through devotion, service, and remembrance of the divine Name.

### Jainism

Every living being, from humans to the smallest microorganism, has a soul (_jiva_). The soul is eternal, capable of infinite knowledge, perception, bliss, and energy, but is weighed down by karmic matter (_ajiva_). Liberation (_moksha_) comes through right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct, literally _shedding_ the accumulated karmic particles. Jainism's radical commitment to non-violence (_ahimsa_) flows from this: harming any living being is harming a soul.

### Daoism

The Daoist tradition speaks of _hun_ (the ethereal, yang soul that ascends at death) and _po_ (the corporeal, yin soul that descends). Each person has three _hun_ and seven _po_. Rather than separating soul from body, Daoism seeks their harmony through practices like meditation, qi cultivation, and living in accordance with the Dao. The goal is not escape from the body but the refinement and transformation of the whole person, body, energy, and spirit united.

### African Traditional Religions

Diverse across the continent, but common themes include: multiple souls or soul-aspects (the Yoruba recognize _emi_, breath/spirit, _ori_, personal destiny/consciousness, and _ojiji_, shadow); ancestral continuity (the dead remain connected to the living); and the vital force (_ashe_ in Yoruba, _nyama_ in Mande) that flows through all existence.

## Patterns Across Traditions

Despite vast differences, several patterns emerge:

1. **Soul-as-breath** is nearly universal: _psyche_, _nephesh_, _ruach_, _ruh_, _atman_ (related to German _atmen_, to breathe), _prana_, _qi_. The connection of soul to breath is the oldest layer of meaning.

2. **The soul connects more than it separates.** Even traditions that emphasize the soul's distinctness (Christianity, Islam) frame it as what connects humans to God. Traditions that emphasize interconnection (animism, Buddhism, Hinduism) see soul as the medium of relationship.

3. **Moral dimension is central.** Across traditions, the soul bears moral weight, it's what makes ethical life possible and meaningful. The soul can be cultivated, damaged, purified, or corrupted.

4. **The body is not the enemy.** While Platonic and Gnostic dualism cast the body as a prison, most traditions are more nuanced. Judaism, Islam, Indigenous traditions, Daoism, and even Christianity (through the doctrine of resurrection) affirm the body's importance.

5. **The question resists final answers.** The Quran says the _ruh_ is beyond human knowledge. The Buddha refused to answer whether the self exists or doesn't. Kant declared the soul unknowable. The deepest traditions recognize that the question may be more important than any answer.
